## Section 2.1 (Governance) and 3.1 (Financing) — targeted reliability assessment

### Section 2.1 — Governance

**What is well-covered and citable:**
- The ownership chain (Shanghai SASAC → SAIC Group → SAIC Motor listed) is thoroughly documented with PwC-audited primary sources. The AR2025 source page has the CCP leadership clause verbatim (p. 35), the strongest primary-source citation for the party-control argument.
- Seven ABS 4* academic sources (Shleifer & Vishny, Fan et al., Jia et al., Megginson & Netter, Bortolotti & Faccio, Estrin et al., Bai et al.) are fully ingested with page numbers. The source-rating.md provides clear guidance on how to deploy each.
- The EU tariff differential (SAIC 35.3% vs. BYD 17.0%) is primary-source verified and provides an external independent benchmark for state subsidy reliance.

**Risks for Section 2.1:**
1. The [[entities/saic-motor]] page quotes the SAIC Group stake as 63.27% (AR2024) but the AR2025 page shows 63.71%. If you cite the entity page rather than the source pages directly, you will use the stale number.
2. The AR2025 governance section notes the Supervisory Board (Aufsichtsrat) has been abolished under China's new company law (2025), with the Audit Committee taking over. This structural governance change is not captured on any entity or concept page. It is directly relevant to Section 2.1's discussion of oversight mechanisms.
3. The `state-owned-enterprise` concept page still references Bruton et al. (2015) in its academic sources table even though that source page was deleted in the refactor. The reference in the table itself is probably still citable (the published paper exists; only the wiki source page was removed), but the wiki entry creates confusion about whether this source is "in" or "out" of the source set.
4. The broken links `[[china-ev-price-war]]` on the saic-motor, saic-gm, and saic-volkswagen entity pages are harmless for citations but indicate incomplete page graphs.

### Section 3.1 — Financing

**What is well-covered and citable:**
- The capital structure of the JV model (capital-light via co-investment, now exposed to 50% impairment losses) is well-documented in both annual reports with specific figures: SAIC-GM impairment wiped >RMB 30bn in 2024; operating cash flow +63.62% driven by inventory reduction (AR2024).
- Capacity utilization data (AR2025) — SAIC-GM 37%, SAIC-VW 55%, MEB plant 14% — are the strongest quantitative indicators of idle capital. These are in the AR2025 source page but not yet in any entity page.
- The Megginson & Netter (2001) benchmark (+3.96pp profitability, −4.69pp leverage post-privatization) and DeWenter & Malatesta global SOE ROS/ROE figures are referenced in the source-rating guide; however, the DeWenter & Malatesta source page was deleted. The Quellen-Übersicht.md still has a full entry for D&M as if it exists.
- The Estrin et al. (2009) "foreign > domestic > insider/state" ownership hierarchy is fully ingested and relevant for explaining why the JV (foreign ownership at entity level) outperformed SAIC-owned brands (state ownership).

**Risks for Section 3.1:**
1. **DeWenter & Malatesta (2001):** The Quellen-Übersicht.md (Section "Thema 4", Item "DeWenter & Malatesta") still has a full entry describing it as a primary source for the Financing dimension. The source page was deleted. If you follow the Quellen-Übersicht.md as a writing guide, you may attempt to cite a wiki page that does not exist. The published paper (AER 2001) can still be cited; only the wiki page was removed. Low risk if you cite the journal directly, high risk if you follow the source-rating table which still lists it.
2. **NEV subsidy conflict (AR2025):** The AR2025 source page has a `⚠️ Conflict` callout: NEV subsidies = 0 per AR2025 (p. 21) vs. EU Reg. 2024/2754 classifying SAIC as heavily subsidized. This conflict is documented but unresolved. For Section 3.1, this matters: you cannot simultaneously claim "SAIC receives no state subsidies" (AR self-report) and "SAIC received the highest EU countervailing duty because of subsidies" (EU primary source) without explicitly noting the definitional difference (product-level cash subsidies vs. production-cost/infrastructure subsidies). The conflict callout correctly identifies this, but the concept pages do not address it.
3. The R&D figures differ between years: AR2024 states RMB 35.16bn total R&D (+7.32%); AR2025 states RMB 33.58bn (−5.0%). This is a factual inconsistency between two primary sources that is not flagged anywhere as a conflict. It may reflect a methodological change in what is included in "total R&D incl. JVs." Not currently documented.
