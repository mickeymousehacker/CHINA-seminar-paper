# LFP Chemistry

**Lithium iron phosphate (LFP) battery chemistry using iron and phosphate as cathode materials instead of nickel and cobalt — safer, cheaper, and longer-lasting than nickel-based chemistries, at the cost of lower energy density.**

## Summary

LFP (LiFePO4) chemistry is the foundational battery technology behind [[byd-company]]'s [[blade-battery]] and the strategic choice underlying BYD's cost leadership position. The use of iron and phosphate instead of nickel, manganese, and cobalt (NMC) or nickel, cobalt, and aluminum (NCA) means LFP cells are inherently thermally and chemically more stable, which eliminates a key safety barrier and removes cobalt — the most price-volatile, ethically contested, and geopolitically concentrated material in the conventional EV battery supply chain. [[2026-05-22-byd-vertical-integration-web-research]]

The classical weakness of LFP is lower energy density compared to nickel-based chemistries. BYD's cell-to-pack (CTP) [[blade-battery]] design specifically addresses this constraint: by using the elongated blade geometry to maximize volumetric packing efficiency within the structural pack, BYD achieves 439–450 Wh/L volumetric density despite LFP's lower intrinsic energy density — enabling competitive range without chemistry compromise. [[2026-05-22-byd-vertical-integration-web-research]]

LFP's longevity advantage (>5,000 charge cycles vs. typically 1,000–2,000 for NMC) significantly lowers total cost of ownership, making it the superior choice for mass-market vehicles where total ownership economics dominate the purchase decision. [[2026-05-22-byd-vertical-integration-web-research]]
