# EV Price War

**The intensifying price competition in China's electric vehicle market from 2023 onward, driven by overcapacity, 129+ competing brands, subsidy phaseout, and BYD's deliberate use of price cuts as a market-share weapon — compressing margins across the industry and testing the resilience of different governance/supply-chain models.**

## Summary

China's EV price war is a structural consequence of several converging forces: (1) rapid industry capacity expansion driven by government policy and private investment; (2) the phaseout of China's national EV purchase subsidies at end-2022, which removed demand-side cushioning; (3) 129+ Chinese EV brands competing for a finite market, many operating at losses; and (4) [[byd-company]]'s deliberate deployment of price cuts as a market-share weapon, enabled by its [[vertical-integration]]-derived cost floor. [[2026-05-22-byd-vertical-integration-web-research]]

BYD slashed prices on 22 models by up to 34% in 2025 to maintain volume growth, driving international deliveries up 144.7% YoY but compressing domestic margins severely. BYD's gross margin trajectory illustrates the dynamic:
- 2023 full-year gross margin: 21.4% (peak)
- 2024 full-year gross margin: 19.4% (still record-high for the year, but compressing through the year)
- Q1 2024 gross margin: 17.3%
- Q2 2025 gross margin: 10–15% (below Tesla's 18% and Li Auto's 20.1%)
- Q2 2025 net profit: down 29.9% YoY despite 14% revenue growth [[2026-05-22-byd-vertical-integration-web-research]]

For [[byd-company]], vertical integration provides two distinct competitive responses to the price war: (1) a lower operational cost floor than competitors dependent on external suppliers, meaning BYD can sustain aggressive pricing at margins where external-sourced competitors lose money; (2) the ability to absorb margin pressure that would be existentially threatening to less-integrated rivals. However, the price war also tests the overcapacity risk inherent in deep vertical integration — if volumes decline, BYD's fixed-cost base in manufacturing assets is harder to flex than a fee-for-service supplier arrangement.
