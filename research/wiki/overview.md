# Wiki Overview

**Research focus: BYD Company Limited and the Chinese automotive industry governance models comparison (Hausarbeit).**

## Current thesis

The available evidence supports a nuanced thesis: **[[byd-company]]'s vertically integrated, founder-led governance model has generated superior long-term shareholder value compared to JV or SOE models in the EV transition — but this advantage is neither free nor permanent.**

The model's structural advantages are well-documented: 75–80% in-house component share creates a 30–40% battery cost advantage; the [[blade-battery]] is a proprietary technology moat requiring 1,117 patents; [[founder-led-governance]] enables R&D reinvestment at rates impossible under dispersed ownership (R&D > net profit for 13/14 years); and supply chain control proved decisive during the 2021–2022 chip shortage. The [[berkshire-hathaway]] 17-year investment returned 3,890%, providing an external validation data point.

The model's risks are equally structural: margin compression from the [[ev-price-war]] (gross margin fell from 21.4% in 2023 to 10–15% by Q2 2025); geopolitical tariff exposure (EU +17%, US 100%); overcapacity risk in fixed manufacturing assets; and the conflict-of-interest constraint on [[findreams]] Battery's external sales. The embedded CCP cell in BYD's corporate structure per Article 19 adds governance opacity.

For the comparative paper question ("which model creates most shareholder value?"), the BYD data suggests the private vertically-integrated model outperforms JV and SOE models in the EV transition era — but the answer may be conditional: BYD's model requires a founder of Wang Chuanfu's technical depth and long-term vision, combined with a product domain (batteries) that happens to be the core value driver of the entire EV transition.

## Active threads

1. **SOE and JV model data gap** — The wiki currently lacks deep coverage of FAW, SAIC, Dongfeng (SOE) or specific JV partnerships. These are needed for the comparative paper. Suggested next ingests: Rhodium Group "Hangover" report on foreign carmakers' China strategies; Harvard CID JV technology transfer analysis.

2. **ROIC comparison** — No systematic Return on Invested Capital comparison across governance models yet in the wiki. This is the most direct metric for the paper's central question.

3. **Post-2024 margin trajectory** — Q2 2025 gross margin of 10–15% raises questions about whether BYD's vertical integration advantage is being competed away in the price war, or whether the compression is a strategic investment (volume over margin) with a recoverable endpoint.

4. **Subsidy decomposition** — Subsidies explain only ~5% of BYD's cost advantage over Tesla per one source, but BYD received US$2.2 billion in subsidies in 2022 alone. The paper needs to clearly distinguish genuine competitive capability from policy-dependent advantage.
