## SECTION 1: CONFIRMED ABS 4* PAPERS

### Paper 1 — Fan, Wong & Zhang (2007) — JFE 4*

**Full citation**: Fan, J.P.H., Wong, T.J., & Zhang, T. (2007). Politically connected CEOs, corporate governance, and Post-IPO performance of China's newly partially privatized firms. *Journal of Financial Economics*, 84(2), 330–357. https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jfineco.2006.03.008

**Source angle**: [[01-fan-wong-zhang-political-ceos-jfe]]

**Journal**: Journal of Financial Economics — **ABS 4***

**Confirmed published**: Yes — AEA/Elsevier peer-reviewed journal; multiple academic database records (IDEAS/RePEC eee:jfinec, Oxford Academic, EconPapers, author's own page).

**Key findings** (verbatim from search results): Almost 27% of the CEOs in a sample of 790 newly partially privatized firms in China are former or current government bureaucrats. Firms with politically connected CEOs underperform those without politically connected CEOs by almost 18% based on three-year post-IPO stock returns and have poorer three-year post-IPO earnings growth, sales growth, and change in returns on sales. The negative effect of the CEO's political ties also show up in the first-day stock return.

**Relevance to SAIC Motor governance dimension**: Directly applicable — SAIC Motor's CEO and board appointments are controlled by Shanghai SASAC and the CCP Shanghai Municipal Committee. This paper quantifies the performance cost of politically connected executives in Chinese partially privatized firms, providing the key empirical anchor for the argument that state ownership and party governance affect SAIC's decision-making and performance incentives.

### Paper 2 — Shleifer & Vishny (1994) — QJE 4*

**Full citation**: Shleifer, A., & Vishny, R.W. (1994). Politicians and firms. *The Quarterly Journal of Economics*, 109(4), 995–1025. https://doi.org/10.2307/2118354

**Source angle**: [[03-shleifer-vishny-politicians-firms-qje]]

**Journal**: The Quarterly Journal of Economics — **ABS 4***

**Confirmed published**: Yes — Oxford University Press peer-reviewed journal; Oxford Academic page confirms Vol. 109, No. 4, November 1994, pp. 995–1025, DOI 10.2307/2118354.

**Key findings** (verbatim from search results): Presents a model of bargaining between politicians and managers that explains stylized facts about state firms, their commercialization, and privatization. The model shows how subsidies to public enterprises and bribes from managers to politicians emerge naturally, and uses it to understand why commercialization and privatization might work and what forces contribute to effective restructuring of public enterprises.

**Relevance to SAIC Motor governance dimension**: Provides the foundational theoretical model for politician–manager bargaining in state enterprises. Explains why Shanghai SASAC retains control over SAIC even post-listing and why SAIC's management serves political objectives (employment, industrial policy) alongside profit maximization.

### Paper 3 — Megginson & Netter (2001) — JEL 4*

**Full citation**: Megginson, W.L., & Netter, J.M. (2001). From state to market: A survey of empirical studies on privatization. *Journal of Economic Literature*, 39(2), 321–389. https://doi.org/10.1257/jel.39.2.321

**Source angle**: [[04-megginson-netter-privatization-jel]]

**Journal**: Journal of Economic Literature — **ABS 4***

**Confirmed published**: Yes — AEA peer-reviewed journal; AEA website confirms DOI 10.1257/jel.39.2.321, Vol. 39, No. 2, June 2001, pp. 321–389.

**Key findings** (verbatim from search results): Surveys the literature examining the privatization of SOEs; reviews the history of privatization, the theoretical and empirical evidence on the relative performance of state owned and privately owned firms, the types of privatization, if and by how much privatization has improved the performance of former SOEs in non-transition and transition countries, how investors in privatizations have fared, and the impact of privatization on the development of capital markets and corporate governance. In most settings privatization "works" in that the firms become more efficient, more profitable, and financially healthier, and reward investors.

**Relevance to SAIC Motor governance dimension**: Establishes the empirical baseline that full state ownership depresses performance relative to privatized firms. SAIC Motor (listed but majority state-owned via Shanghai SASAC) is precisely the "partial privatization" case this survey evaluates — relevant for framing why SAIC's governance model is a contested middle ground.

### Paper 4 — Bortolotti & Faccio (2009) — RFS 4*

**Full citation**: Bortolotti, B., & Faccio, M. (2009). Government control of privatized firms. *Review of Financial Studies*, 22(8), 2907–2939. https://doi.org/10.1093/rfs/hhn077

**Source angle**: [[05-bortolotti-faccio-govt-control-rfs]]

**Journal**: Review of Financial Studies — **ABS 4***

**Confirmed published**: Yes — Oxford University Press peer-reviewed journal; Oxford Academic page confirms Vol. 22, Issue 8, August 2009, pp. 2907–2939.

**Key findings** (verbatim from search results): At the end of 2000, after the largest privatization wave in history, governments retained control of 62.4% of privatized firms. In civil law countries, governments tend to retain large ownership positions, whereas in common law countries they typically use golden shares. More prevalent government influence over privatized firms appears in countries with proportional electoral rules and with a centralized system of political authority.

**Relevance to SAIC Motor governance dimension**: Directly relevant — China is a civil-law country with centralized political authority; the paper predicts that Shanghai SASAC would retain large ownership stakes even after SAIC's partial listing, which is precisely what occurred. Supports the argument that SAIC's "privatization" did not produce genuine transfer of control.

### Paper 5 — Dewenter & Malatesta (2001) — AER 4*

**Full citation**: DeWenter, K.L., & Malatesta, P.H. (2001). State-owned and privately owned firms: An empirical analysis of profitability, leverage, and labor intensity. *American Economic Review*, 91(1), 320–334. https://doi.org/10.1257/aer.91.1.320

**Source angle**: [[06-dewenter-malatesta-state-private-aer]]

**Journal**: American Economic Review — **ABS 4***

**Confirmed published**: Yes — AEA peer-reviewed journal; AEA website confirms DOI 10.1257/aer.91.1.320, Vol. 91, No. 1, March 2001, pp. 320–334.

**Key findings** (verbatim from search results): Study examines the 500 largest non-US firms. Shows that state-owned enterprises are leveraged more, and that leverage falls after privatisation.

**Relevance to SAIC Motor governance dimension**: Provides empirical evidence on profitability and leverage differentials between SOEs and private firms, applicable to SAIC Motor's financial structure and its exposure to political objectives (employment, market share, JV quotas) rather than pure profit maximization.

### Paper 6 — Estrin, Hanousek, Kočenda & Svejnar (2009) — JEL 4*

**Full citation**: Estrin, S., Hanousek, J., Kočenda, E., & Svejnar, J. (2009). The effects of privatization and ownership in transition economies. *Journal of Economic Literature*, 47(3), 699–728. https://doi.org/10.1257/jel.47.3.699

**Source angle**: [[12-estrin-privatization-transition-jel]]

**Journal**: Journal of Economic Literature — **ABS 4***

**Confirmed published**: Yes — AEA peer-reviewed journal; AEA website confirms DOI 10.1257/jel.47.3.699, Vol. 47, No. 3, September 2009, pp. 699–728.

**Key findings** (verbatim from search results): In post-communist economies privatization to foreign owners results in a rapid improvement in performance of firms, while performance effects of privatization to domestic owners are less impressive and vary across regions. Concentrated private ownership has a stronger positive effect on performance than dispersed ownership in the post-communist economies.

**Relevance to SAIC Motor governance dimension**: China's SOE reform shares structural features with post-communist transition economies. SAIC's partial listing without genuine privatization exemplifies the "domestic insider" privatization pattern that Estrin et al. find yields the weakest performance improvements. Foreign-partner JV involvement (GM, VW) can be framed as one mechanism by which SAIC access performance improvements without full privatization.

### Paper 7 — Yan & Gray (1994) — AMJ 4*

**Full citation**: Yan, A., & Gray, B. (1994). Bargaining power, management control, and performance in United States–China joint ventures: A comparative case study. *Academy of Management Journal*, 37(6), 1478–1517. https://doi.org/10.5465/256796

**Source angle**: [[08-yan-gray-bargaining-power-amj]]

**Journal**: Academy of Management Journal — **ABS 4***

**Confirmed published**: Yes — AOM peer-reviewed journal; AOM Journals page confirms DOI 10.5465/256796, Vol. 37, No. 6, December 1994, pp. 1478–1517.

**Key findings** (verbatim from search results): Reports a comparative case study of four joint ventures between partners from the United States and the People's Republic of China. The bargaining power of potential partners affects the structure of management control in a joint venture, which affects venture performance. Several informal control mechanisms interacting with formal control structure and influencing performance are identified, and an integrative model of management control in joint ventures is presented.

**Relevance to SAIC Motor governance dimension**: Directly applicable to SAIC Motor's JV governance model. SAIC is the Chinese parent in multiple 50/50 JVs (SAIC-GM, SAIC-VW). Yan & Gray's framework explains how SAIC and its foreign JV partners exercise management control through formal (equity stakes) and informal (personnel, technology access) mechanisms — and how bargaining power shapes governance outcomes.

### Paper 8 — Jia, Huang & Zhang (2019) — AMJ 4*

**Full citation**: Jia, N., Huang, K.G., & Zhang, C.M. (2019). Public governance, corporate governance, and firm innovation: An examination of state-owned enterprises. *Academy of Management Journal*, 62(1), 220–247. https://doi.org/10.5465/amj.2016.0543

**Source angle**: [[09-china-soe-governance-smj-amj-asq]]

**Journal**: Academy of Management Journal — **ABS 4***

**Confirmed published**: Yes — AOM peer-reviewed journal; AOM Journals canonical page confirms DOI 10.5465/amj.2016.0543, Vol. 62, Issue 1, 2019, pp. 220–247. Also confirmed by SSRN preprint record (3165690) and Singapore Management University institutional repository.

**Key findings** (from SSRN/Semantic Scholar records): Examines the interaction between public governance (state-level) and corporate governance (firm-level) on firm innovation in SOEs. Distinguishes between central and local state ownership effects.

**Relevance to SAIC Motor governance dimension**: Directly addresses the dual-governance problem faced by SAIC — subject to both public governance (Shanghai SASAC, CCP) and corporate governance (board, listed-company rules). Tests how these interact to shape strategic decisions including innovation, a live issue for SAIC's EV strategy under both state ownership and JV technology constraints.

### Paper 9 — Cuervo-Cazurra, Inkpen, Musacchio & Ramaswamy (2014) — JIBS ⚠️ RANKING UNCERTAIN

**Full citation**: Cuervo-Cazurra, A., Inkpen, A., Musacchio, A., & Ramaswamy, K. (2014). Governments as owners: State-owned multinational companies. *Journal of International Business Studies*, 45(8), 919–942. https://doi.org/10.1057/jibs.2014.43

**Source angle**: [[02-cuervo-cazurra-governments-as-owners-jibs]]

**Journal**: Journal of International Business Studies

**ABS Ranking**: ⚠️ UNCERTAIN — AJG 2024 most likely 4* (Chartered ABS official source, confirmed by one search result describing it as "4* journal"); AJG 2021 was ABS 4. A second search result gave 3*, but this appears to be an error or outdated source. Recommend treating as ABS 4 (conservative) or verifying against the official AJG 2024 PDF. **Do not rely on this ranking without verification.**

**Confirmed published**: Yes — Palgrave Macmillan for Academy of International Business; IDEAS/RePEC record pal/jintbs/v45y2014i8p919-942; Harvard DASH repository; CBS Research Portal.

**Key findings** (verbatim from search results): The paper explains how the analysis of state-owned multinational companies (SOMNCs) can help advance the international business literature by extending our understanding of state-owned firms (SOEs) and multinational companies (MNCs). The authors introduce two key arguments: the extraterritoriality argument and the non-business internationalization argument, which help explain how SOMNCs extend both the SOE and MNC literatures respectively.

**Relevance to SAIC Motor governance dimension**: SAIC Motor is expanding internationally (MG Motor UK, acquired GM's Indian operations). Cuervo-Cazurra et al. provide the theoretical framework for how state ownership shapes internationalization strategies — explaining why SAIC's overseas moves differ from privately-owned MNCs in terms of risk tolerance, host-country reception, and governance structure.

### Paper 10 — Luo, Shenkar & Nyaw (2001) — JIBS ⚠️ RANKING UNCERTAIN (same journal as above)

**Full citation**: Luo, Y., Shenkar, O., & Nyaw, M. (2001). A dual parent perspective on control and performance in international joint ventures: Lessons from a developing economy. *Journal of International Business Studies*, 32(1), 41–58. https://doi.org/10.1057/palgrave.jibs.8490937

**Source angle**: [[07-luo-shenkar-nyaw-ijv-jibs]]

**Journal**: Journal of International Business Studies

**ABS Ranking**: ⚠️ Same JIBS ranking uncertainty as Paper 9 above. Treat as ABS 4 conservative; likely 4* in AJG 2024.

**Confirmed published**: Yes — Palgrave Macmillan for Academy of International Business; SpringerLink canonical page DOI 10.1057/palgrave.jibs.8490937; IDEAS/RePEC record pal/jintbs/v32y2001i1p41-58.

**Key findings** (verbatim from search results): The study compares the control-performance relationship for foreign versus local parents in international joint ventures in China, finding that transaction cost analysis is more applicable to foreign than to Chinese parents, with both overall and specific controls associated with performance for foreign parents, while only specific control is for Chinese parents. Under goal incongruity, Chinese parent control is associated with foreign parent dissatisfaction with venture performance, but not vice-versa, and having a majority stake improves performance from a foreign parent perspective, but not from that of the Chinese parent.

**Relevance to SAIC Motor governance dimension**: Directly applicable to SAIC Motor's 50/50 JVs. The "dual parent" framework captures the asymmetric governance positions of SAIC (state-owned, local parent) and GM/VW (foreign parents). The finding that Chinese parent control is associated with foreign parent dissatisfaction maps precisely to documented tensions in SAIC's JVs over technology transfer, EV strategy, and local market adaptation.

### Paper 11 — Bruton, Peng, Ahlstrom, Stan & Xu (2015) — AMP ABS 4

**Full citation**: Bruton, G.D., Peng, M.W., Ahlstrom, D., Stan, C., & Xu, K. (2015). State-owned enterprises around the world as hybrid organizations. *Academy of Management Perspectives*, 29(1), 92–114. https://doi.org/10.5465/amp.2013.0069

**Source angle**: [[11-bruton-peng-ahlstrom-hybrid-amp]]

**Journal**: Academy of Management Perspectives — **ABS 4** (not 4*)

**Confirmed published**: Yes — AOM peer-reviewed journal; AOM Journals canonical page confirms DOI 10.5465/amp.2013.0069, Vol. 29, Issue 1, February 2015, pp. 92–114. Note: DOI suffix shows 2013 (submission year), publication year is 2015.

**Key findings** (verbatim from search results): State-owned enterprises represent approximately 10% of global gross domestic product but remain relatively underexplored by management scholars. The paper argues for viewing firms not dichotomously as either state owned or privately owned, but rather as hybrid organizations in which the levels of ownership and control by the state can vary. The research draws on 36 cases from four industries in 23 countries to provide a richer understanding of state-owned enterprises.

**Relevance to SAIC Motor governance dimension**: The hybrid organization framework is arguably the most directly applicable theoretical lens for SAIC Motor — listed on Shanghai Stock Exchange (market governance), majority state-owned by Shanghai SASAC (state governance), and operating JVs with private MNCs (market governance). Bruton et al.'s framework captures this complexity without forcing a binary state/private classification.
