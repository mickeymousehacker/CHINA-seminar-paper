## Result 1 — PRIORITY 1

- **Authors**: Jie Bai, Panle Jia Barwick, Shengmao Cao, Shanjun Li
- **Title**: "Quid Pro Quo, Knowledge Spillovers, and Industrial Quality Upgrading: Evidence from the Chinese Auto Industry"
- **Journal**: American Economic Review
- **Volume/Issue/Pages**: Vol. 115, No. 11, pp. 3825–52
- **Year**: 2025
- **DOI**: 10.1257/aer.20221501
- **ABS/VHB Ranking**: ABS 4* (highest), VHB A+
- **URL**: https://www.aeaweb.org/articles?id=10.1257/aer.20221501
- **Publication status**: CONFIRMED published — verified via AEAweb.org
- **Priority**: Priorität 1

**Content** (verbatim from search results and AEA page):

Foreign automakers are required to set up joint ventures with domestic automakers in return for market access in the Chinese automobile industry. This paper provides evidence on whether forced technology transfer via joint ventures can accelerate industrial upgrading. Affiliated domestic automakers tend to adopt the quality strengths of their JV partners, consistent with knowledge spillovers. The findings show that affiliated domestic automakers adopt more similar quality strengths of their joint venture partners, consistent with knowledge spillovers, with worker flows and supplier networks mediating knowledge spillovers. Knowledge spillovers due to ownership affiliation under quid pro quo contributed 8.3 percent of the quality improvement experienced by affiliated domestic models from 2001 to 2014, relative to nonaffiliated domestic models. Affiliated firms scoring 0.098 standard deviations higher when a JV model scored one standard deviation higher.

**Relevance summary**: Directly evaluates whether China's quid-pro-quo JV policy (the exact policy framework that built SAIC's business model) actually produced technology transfer and quality upgrading in affiliated domestic OEMs. The 8.3% quality-improvement figure and the worker-flow/supplier-network mechanism are directly applicable to analyzing SAIC-VW and SAIC-GM capability building outcomes. Published in the top-ranked economics journal.

## Result 2 — PRIORITY 1

- **Authors**: Thomas J. Holmes, Ellen R. McGrattan, Edward C. Prescott
- **Title**: "Quid Pro Quo: Technology Capital Transfers for Market Access in China"
- **Journal**: Review of Economic Studies (Oxford University Press)
- **Volume/Issue/Pages**: Vol. 82, No. 3 (July 2015), pp. 1154–1193
- **Year**: 2015
- **DOI**: 10.1093/restud/rdv007
- **ABS/VHB Ranking**: ABS 4*, VHB A+
- **URL**: https://academic.oup.com/restud/article-abstract/82/3/1154/1575435 | JSTOR: https://www.jstor.org/stable/43551732
- **Publication status**: CONFIRMED published — verified via Oxford Academic and IDEAS/RePEC
- **Priority**: Priorität 1

**Content** (verbatim from search results):

China's quid pro quo policy exchanges market access for transfers of technology capital—accumulated know-how such as research and development (R&D) that can be used in multiple production locations. The research finds large welfare gains for China and welfare losses for its FDI partners from quid pro quo. By the 1970s, quid pro quo policy, which requires multinational firms to transfer technology in return for market access, had become a common practice in many developing countries. The authors assess global capital flows in light of China's quid pro quo policy of exchanging market access for transfers of technology capital. The paper finds large welfare gains for China and welfare losses for its FDI partners from quid pro quo.

**Relevance summary**: Provides the theoretical macroeconomic framework for the market-access-for-technology-transfer policy that shaped SAIC's entire JV structure with VW and GM. The welfare analysis (gains for China, losses for FDI partners) frames the Governance and Financing dimensions of the paper.

## Result 3 — PRIORITY 1

- **Authors**: Sabrina T. Howell
- **Title**: "Joint ventures and technology adoption: A Chinese industrial policy that backfired"
- **Journal**: Research Policy
- **Volume/Issue/Pages**: Vol. 47, No. 8, pp. 1448–1462
- **Year**: 2018
- **DOI**: 10.1016/j.respol.2018.05.002
- **ABS/VHB Ranking**: ABS 3, VHB B
- **URL**: https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0048733318301124
- **Publication status**: CONFIRMED published — verified via ScienceDirect and IDEAS/RePEC (v47y2018i8p1448-1462)
- **Priority**: Priorität 1

**Content** (verbatim from search results):

To spur technology transfer, emerging market policymakers often require foreign firms to form joint ventures (JVs) with domestic firms. Through knowledge spillovers, JVs may reduce technology acquisition costs for domestic firms. Yet domestic firm rents from JVs could discourage innovation through a cannibalization effect. The paper is based on novel, national, model-level data of light-duty passenger vehicle sales in China between 1999 and 2013. In response to fuel economy standards requiring firms to upgrade technology or sacrifice quality, firms with JVs reduced quality and price relative to their counterparts. Consistent with cannibalization, firms with JVs drive the negative effect.

**Relevance summary**: Directly contradicts the optimistic reading of the quid-pro-quo model — argues that JV rents actually REDUCED innovation incentives for domestic Chinese OEMs (cannibalization effect), which speaks directly to the Long-term Viability question for SAIC. The 1999–2013 dataset covers the exact period of SAIC's JV-dependent growth.

## Result 4 — PRIORITY 2

- **Authors**: Andrew C. Inkpen, Paul W. Beamish
- **Title**: "Knowledge, Bargaining Power, and the Instability of International Joint Ventures"
- **Journal**: Academy of Management Review
- **Volume/Issue/Pages**: Vol. 22, No. 1, pp. 177–202
- **Year**: 1997
- **DOI**: 10.5465/amr.1997.9707180263
- **ABS/VHB Ranking**: ABS 4*, VHB A+
- **URL**: https://journals.aom.org/doi/abs/10.5465/amr.1997.9707180263
- **Publication status**: CONFIRMED published — verified via journals.aom.org and multiple citation sources
- **Priority**: Priorität 2

**Content** (verbatim from search results):

The instability of IJVs is associated with shifts in partner bargaining power, which occur when partners acquire sufficient knowledge and skills to eliminate a partner dependency and make the IJV bargain obsolete. Conflict, confrontations and crises seem unavoidable in JVs, leading to poor performance, threatening failure, and even dissolution. Inter-partner learning of each other's competencies during operation of the enterprise is an important reason for JV instability. Specifically, the JV is likely to break up if the learning effect is stronger than the synergy effect. When the collaborative basis of a venture shifts, perhaps due to changes in parent firms' strategies, transaction costs from renegotiations can increase and adversely affect the relationship. International joint ventures confront high chances of termination, and termination can occur in different ways: by dissolving the venture or by having one of the partners fully acquire the venture, with these different modes of dissolution prompted by different causes.

**Relevance summary**: Core IB theory on JV instability directly applicable to SAIC's JV situation — as Chinese domestic capabilities improve and the regulatory quid-pro-quo disappears (2022), the knowledge-based dependency rationale erodes. Relevant to Capability Building and Long-term Viability dimensions.

## Result 5 — PRIORITY 2

- **Authors**: Kyle Chan
- **Title**: "Inside China's state-owned enterprises: Managed competition through a multi-level structure"
- **Journal**: Competition & Change (SAGE / Chinese Journal of Sociology imprint)
- **Volume/Year**: 2022
- **DOI**: 10.1177/2057150X221123388
- **ABS/VHB Ranking**: ABS 2
- **URL**: https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/full/10.1177/2057150X221123388
- **Publication status**: CONFIRMED published — verified via SAGE journals page
- **Priority**: Priorität 2

**Content** (verbatim from search results):

The article describes a system of "managed competition" among China's state-owned enterprises that attempts to harness the forces of competition while intervening to ensure a robust field of capable competitors, implemented through a multi-level structure where parent state-owned enterprises coordinate and balance competition among a set of similarly resourced subsidiaries through the allocation of management personnel and resources. The article examines how this approach works in practice through an in-depth empirical case study of two of China's largest infrastructure construction firms: China Railway Group Limited and China Railway Construction Corporation. In the marketization era, the state still expects SOEs to preserve state assets and fulfill political goals, though SOEs do not generally perform as well as private corporations worldwide.

**Relevance summary**: Provides a theoretical framework for understanding how SAIC, as a SASAC-supervised SOE, is structured — the "managed competition" model explains why SAIC maintains both SAIC-VW and SAIC-GM simultaneously and how party-state oversight operates. Directly relevant to Governance dimension.

## Result 7 — PRIORITY 2

- **Authors**: Jiangyu Wang, Cheng Han Tan
- **Title**: "Mixed Ownership Reform and Corporate Governance in China's State-Owned Enterprises"
- **Journal**: Vanderbilt Journal of Transnational Law
- **Volume/Issue**: Vol. 53, No. 3
- **Year**: 2020
- **URL**: https://scholarship.law.vanderbilt.edu/vjtl/vol53/iss3/9/
- **Publication status**: CONFIRMED published — verified via Vanderbilt Law scholarship repository
- **Priority**: Priorität 2

**Content** (verbatim from search results):

The paper assesses the impact on corporate governance of China's mixed-ownership reform (MOR) announced by the CCP in 2013. The introduction of nonstate capital into SOEs has led to greater participation in corporate governance by nonstate investors. MOR's impact on SOE corporate governance has been embodied in the "retreat of the state," the "advance of the Chinese Communist Party," and a limited yet emerging separation of power between the Party and the board in SOEs. In SOEs with survival-driven motives, deeper reform significantly improves firm performance, while reforms motivated primarily by policy directives demonstrate weaker effects on performance. The involvement of private shareholders in corporate governance substantially enhances enterprises' propensity for risk-taking in outward foreign direct investment.

**Relevance summary**: Directly addresses SAIC's governance evolution under Xi Jinping-era SOE reform — the paradox of party "advance" alongside "retreat of the state" is central to understanding SAIC's governance dimension. Also addresses financing/capital structure implications of mixed ownership.

## Result 15 — PRIORITY 2

- **Title**: "Industrial Policy, Chinese-Style: FDI, Regulation, and Dreams of National Champions in the Auto Sector"
- **Journal**: Journal of East Asian Studies (Cambridge Core)
- **URL**: https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/journal-of-east-asian-studies/article/abs/industrial-policy-chinesestyle-fdi-regulation-and-dreams-of-national-champions-in-the-auto-sector/260CD6C5577A320D32241F4D7C257B2D
- **ABS/VHB Ranking**: ABS 2
- **Publication status**: CONFIRMED published — Cambridge Core
- **Priority**: Priorität 2
- **Note**: Author names not returned by search — verify on Cambridge Core page

**Content** (verbatim from search results):

In 1979, the first Law on Sino-Foreign Equity Joint Ventures was issued and then updated in 1983 and 1988. During the 1980s and 1990s, foreign carmakers were allowed to form joint ventures (JVs) only with SOEs chosen by the Chinese government, in what was known as "obligated embeddedness," with the first wave dominated by European automobile companies including Volkswagen, Peugeot, Citroën, Fiat-Iveco, and Mercedes-Benz. In 1994, China's first specific policy for the automotive industry stated that JVs could only be established between a foreign and a Chinese partner, and that the latter had to own a stake of at least 50 percent in the venture. Foreign companies were limited to no more than 50% ownership in these agreements and were also limited to no more than two joint ventures for any single vehicle type produced in China.

**Relevance summary**: Policy history tracing the exact regulatory framework (50/50 JV requirement, two-JV limit) that shaped SAIC's 1984 VW JV and 1997 GM JV. Essential historical context for the Governance dimension.
