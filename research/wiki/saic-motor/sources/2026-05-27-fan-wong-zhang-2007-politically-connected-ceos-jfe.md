# Politically Connected CEOs, Corporate Governance, and Post-IPO Performance of China's Newly Partially Privatized Firms

**Fan, Wong & Zhang (2007) show that 27% of Chinese IPO firms have politically connected CEOs; these firms underperform by ~18% in 3-year post-IPO returns, display weaker accounting performance, and have boards packed with bureaucrats — consistent with the "grabbing hand" theory that politicians extract resources from SOEs at shareholders' expense.**

**Full citation**: Fan, J.P.H., Wong, T.J. and Zhang, T. (2007) 'Politically connected CEOs, corporate governance, and Post-IPO performance of China's newly partially privatized firms', *Journal of Financial Economics*, 84(2), pp. 330–357. https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jfineco.2006.03.008

**Journal**: Journal of Financial Economics — **ABS 4*** (top finance journal)
**Affiliation**: Chinese University of Hong Kong; City University of Hong Kong
**Data**: 790 IPO firms, Shanghai and Shenzhen Stock Exchanges, 1993–2001 (CSMAR database, IPO prospectuses)
**Method**: OLS regressions (post-IPO stock and accounting returns); Poisson regressions (board characteristics)

---

## Key takeaways

- **27% of CEOs** in partially privatized Chinese firms are current or former government bureaucrats — the government retains formal rights to appoint SOE CEOs even after partial privatization. (p. 330, Abstract; p. 335, Table 1)
- **−18% stock underperformance**: Firms with politically connected CEOs underperform politically unconnected peers by ~18% on 3-year post-IPO cumulative abnormal returns (CARs). Specific regression coefficients: −7% (1yr), −10% (2yr), −15% (3yr). (p. 340, Table 3)
- **Accounting performance**: Politically connected firms show −1.6% ROS change, −21% sales growth, −24% earnings growth vs. unconnected peers. (p. 341, Table 4)
- **Board composition**: Boards in politically connected firms have 36% bureaucrat directors vs. 19% in unconnected firms (+17pp); 34% professional directors vs. 47% (−13pp). (p. 350, Table 10)
- **Mechanism**: The "grabbing hand" (Shleifer and Vishny, 1998) — bureaucrats/politicians extract resources from listed SOEs to fulfil objectives inconsistent with firm value maximization. (p. 353, Section 7)
- **Root cause**: Non-transferability of state ownership blocks market-based governance mechanisms (incentive compensation, corporate control); government retains CEO appointment rights. (p. 353)

## Claims (with journal page numbers)

- "Almost 27% of the CEOs in a sample of 790 newly partially privatized firms in China are former or current government bureaucrats." (p. 330, Abstract)
- "Firms with politically connected CEOs underperform those without politically connected CEOs by almost 18% based on three-year post-IPO stock returns and have poorer three-year post-IPO earnings growth, sales growth, and change in returns on sales." (p. 330, Abstract)
- "Firms led by politically connected CEOs are more likely to appoint other bureaucrats to the board of directors rather than directors with relevant professional backgrounds." (p. 330, Abstract)
- Regression coefficients on "CEO is politically connected" dummy: −0.069 (1yr CAR, t=2.87***), −0.099 (2yr, t=2.73***), −0.153 (3yr, t=3.40***). (p. 340, Table 3)
- Regression on accounting performance: −0.016 change in ROS (t=1.94*), −0.208 sales growth (t=2.35**), −0.238 earnings growth (t=1.89*). (p. 341, Table 4)
- Board Poisson regression: politically connected CEO → +60.2% more bureaucrat directors (t=12.01***), −23.1% fewer professional directors (t=5.16***), −66.9% fewer academic directors (t=7.58***). (p. 352, Table 11)
- "The overall evidence is consistent with the 'grabbing hand' argument (Shleifer and Vishny, 1998) that bureaucrats/politicians extract resources from listed SOEs under their control to fulfil objectives that are not consistent with firm value maximization." (p. 353, Section 7)
- "The non-transferability of state-owned shares/assets would create thorny incentive problems... the governance of the firms would likely be weak and firm value would likely be dissipated." (p. 333)
- "Removing these property rights constraints is a key to the success of future reforms aiming to improve the productivity of the corporate sector." (p. 353, Section 7)
- Endogeneity check: negative CAR effect of political connections is robust across high/low GDP, high/low fiscal deficit, high/low unemployment, high/low ROS subsamples. (p. 343, Table 5)
- Effect holds for all three types of connections (central government, local government same region, local government different region), though central government connections show the largest coefficient (−0.285 on 3yr CAR). (p. 344, Table 6)

## Theoretical framework

**"Grabbing hand" vs. "helping hand"**

The paper tests two competing views of government-appointed CEOs in partially privatized firms:

| View | Prediction | Evidence |
|---|---|---|
| **Grabbing hand** (Shleifer & Vishny 1998) | Political connections → rent extraction → poor performance | Confirmed — political connections → −15%/−18% stock/accounting underperformance |
| **Helping hand** | Political connections → regulatory advantages, government contracts → better performance | Rejected by the data |

The property rights mechanism: the state retained (1) non-transferable ownership stakes and (2) CEO appointment rights. This blocked the two governance mechanisms that discipline management in private firms: incentive compensation contracts and market for corporate control.

---

## Empirical design

- **Sample**: 790 A-share IPO firms (73% of all Chinese IPOs 1993–2001); 7,255 CEOs and directors manually coded from IPO prospectuses
- **Political connection definition**: CEO is a current or former officer of central government, local government, or military
- **Performance measures**: 1/2/3-year post-IPO CARs (stock); sales growth, earnings growth, change in ROS (accounting)
- **Board measures**: % bureaucrat directors, % professionals (by sub-type), director age, education, gender

## Relevance for SAIC Motor paper

| Dimension | How to use |
|---|---|
| **Governance** | Core framework: SAIC's CEO is SASAC-appointed — the exact mechanism Fan et al. study. The "grabbing hand" explains why board composition and management incentives at SOEs are tilted toward state/bureaucratic objectives over shareholder value. SAIC's board (dominated by Shanghai SASAC appointees) matches the "high bureaucrats, low professionals" pattern (Table 10, p. 350). |
| **Long-term Viability** | −18% post-IPO underperformance in politically connected firms. SAIC's −88% net profit collapse in 2024 can be situated within this structural pattern — not idiosyncratic bad luck, but predicted by governance structure. Fan et al. show this is systematic across 790 firms. |
| **Financing** | Politically connected firms show −21% sales growth and −24% earnings growth vs. unconnected peers. Government retention of property rights constrains the capital market governance mechanisms that would force management accountability. SAIC's JV dividend reliance is partly a result of this — no market pressure to develop more capital-efficient own-brand businesses. |
| **Capability Building** | Boards with fewer professional directors and more bureaucrats are less likely to champion difficult technology bets. The board composition effect (Table 10/11) provides governance-level evidence for why SAIC was slower to build EV capability than BYD (founder-led, no bureaucrat directors). |
