# Politicians and Firms

**Shleifer & Vishny (1994) show that whether privatization improves firm efficiency depends not on the transfer of cash flow rights but on the transfer of *control* rights: politicians controlling firms maximize political benefit (patronage employment, subsidies) rather than firm value, and the key question is whether managers or politicians hold residual control over employment and Treasury transfers.**

**Full citation**: Shleifer, A. and Vishny, R.W. (1994) 'Politicians and firms', *Quarterly Journal of Economics*, 109(4), pp. 995–1025. JSTOR: 2118354.

**Journal**: Quarterly Journal of Economics — **ABS 4*** (one of the top five economics journals)
**Affiliations**: Andrei Shleifer (Harvard University); Robert W. Vishny (University of Chicago)
**Method**: Theoretical model (incomplete contracts, Nash bargaining); 3-player game: disorganized public, politicians, enterprise managers
**Nature**: Foundational theory paper — no empirical data; develops the formal micro-foundations of the "grabbing hand" model

---

## Key takeaways

- **Control rights, not cash flow rights, determine outcomes**: Under full corruption, the allocation of resources (employment L and Treasury transfers T) is independent of who owns the cash flows — Proposition 1 (p. 1005). Privatization without transfer of control rights changes nothing.
- **Politicians over-employ**: When politicians hold control rights, they push employment L above the socially efficient level to generate political support (votes, patronage). This is the core distortion — not misallocation of profits but misallocation of *inputs* (p. 997–999).
- **Why bribery doesn't fix it**: Bribery reallocates rents between managers and politicians but does not eliminate the fundamental distortion. It can in some cases *promote* restructuring by enabling managers to "buy off" political demands — Proposition 8 (p. 1018). But unrestricted corruption is not an equilibrium — bribes are not legally enforceable.
- **Decency constraint**: In realistic settings, politicians face an upper limit on subsidies (fiscal/political constraint). Under this constraint, potentially profitable firms restructure under manager control, while "hopeless" (deeply unprofitable) firms continue receiving subsidies — Proposition 11/12 (pp. 1019–1020). This predicts selective partial privatization.
- **Politicians prefer manager privatization for good firms**: Politicians support privatizing potentially profitable firms because decency-constrained managers restructure them and reduce the need for Treasury transfers. Politicians prefer keeping loss-making firms under control — Proposition 12 (p. 1020).
- **Politicians prefer weak ownership for managers**: Politicians prefer managers with *low* equity stakes in the firm — because high-stakes managers are better at resisting political demands and extracting fewer subsidies — Proposition 13/14 (p. 1020). This inverts normal governance intuitions.
- **Political competition**: Higher B(L) (political competition / political value of employment) raises equilibrium employment — Proposition 9 (p. 1019). The higher the political payoff from employment, the greater the distortion.
- **Privatization determinants**: Privatization is more likely when (i) the government is conservative (C(T) is high — taxpayers value fiscal restraint) and (ii) B(L) is low (firm's workforce is not politically valuable). These are political conditions, not efficiency conditions.

## Claims (with journal page numbers)

- "We describe a game between the public, the politicians, and the enterprise managers. We assume that, because the public is disorganized, politicians cater to interest groups, such as labor unions, rather than the median voter." (p. 997)
- "Under these assumptions we derive implications of bargaining between politicians and enterprise managers over what managers do. In particular, we focus on the role of transfers between the public and private sector, including subsidies to enterprises and bribes to politicians." (p. 997)
- **Proposition 1** (full corruption): "The allocation of resources is independent of the allocation of cash flow rights between the Treasury and private investors." (p. 1005) — Privatization of cash flows without control transfer is irrelevant.
- **Proposition 2** (politician control): "Under politician control, equilibrium employment is increasing in α [corruption]." (p. 1006)
- **Proposition 3** (politician control, no bribes): "With politician control and no bribes, the no-bribes allocation can have either a higher or a lower T than the jointly efficient point... The manager bribes the politician in the equilibrium with bribes." (p. 1007–1009)
- **Proposition 5** (manager control): "In the no-bribes equilibrium with manager control, employment L is lower and Treasury transfers T are higher than with politician control." (p. 1011) — **This is the core privatization efficiency result.**
- **Proposition 8** (bribery directions): "Bribes from politicians to managers raise L and reduce T. Bribes from managers to politicians raise T and reduce L." (p. 1018) — Bribery can push toward or away from efficiency depending on direction.
- **Proposition 11** (decency constraint, manager control): "With manager control and bribes allowed, the manager chooses zero excess employment [restructures] when the firm is potentially profitable." (p. 1019–1020)
- **Proposition 12** (decency constraint, effect of α): "With manager control, the higher α, the more likely that privatization leads to restructuring." (p. 1020)
- **Proposition 13/14** (politician preferences on management ownership): "Politicians prefer managers who control L to have a low ownership stake." (p. 1020) — Politicians actively resist giving managers strong equity incentives because high-ownership managers resist political demands.
- **Proposition 15** (politician preferences on control): "With bribes, politicians prefer higher private ownership [of cash flows] and lower Treasury transfers compared to manager control." (p. 1021)
- On nationalization of profitable firms: "Politicians want to control firms precisely because such firms have large cash flows from which the Treasury or private shareholders benefit... This is why profitable monopolies are always nationalized or publicly regulated." (p. 1019)
- On privatization determinants: "Privatization is more likely to be politically sustainable when the government is limited in subsidizing profitable firms with rich private owners... and when political benefits from public enterprises are low." (p. 1022–1023)
- On conservative vs. spending governments: "Privatization usually occurs when conservative governments... are favored by taxpayers. For these new governments, B(L) is relatively low, since employees of public companies rarely vote for them, and C(T) is relatively high, because taxpayers do." (p. 1022)

## Theoretical framework

### The model

Three players: (1) the **disorganized public** (taxpayers/voters), (2) **politicians** (maximize votes/political support), (3) **enterprise managers** (maximize personal utility from cash flows).

Two key variables:
- **L** = employment (excess employment is politically valuable but economically costly)
- **T** = Treasury transfer (subsidy from state to firm; costly to taxpayers)

Two political benefit functions:
- **B(L)**: political benefit to politician from excess employment (patronage, union votes)
- **C(T)**: political cost to politician from Treasury transfers (fiscal restraint pressure, taxpayers)

| Control holder | Equilibrium L | Equilibrium T | Efficiency |
|---|---|---|---|
| Politician | **High** (above efficient) | **Low** (politician minimizes T to avoid C(T)) | Poor |
| Manager (no bribes) | Lower | Higher | Better |
| Manager (with decency constraint, profitable firm) | Zero excess employment | Moderate | **Best** |
| Manager (with decency constraint, unprofitable firm) | Positive excess employment | High subsidy | Continues |

### The key irrelevance result (Proposition 1)

Under full corruption (freely negotiated bribes), the allocation of L and T is independent of who owns the cash flows. **Privatization of equity stakes without privatization of control does nothing.** This is the theoretical foundation for Bortolotti & Faccio's empirical finding that 62.4% of "privatized" firms remain government-controlled — because governments only sell cash flow rights, not control rights.

### The governance paradox (Proposition 13)

Politicians prefer managers with **low equity stakes**. This inverts standard principal-agent logic (which prescribes high equity for better incentives). The reason: managers with high equity stakes are better at resisting political demands (they have more to lose from L distortions). Politicians therefore resist governance reforms that strengthen manager incentives — which is exactly what Fan et al. (2007) and Jia et al. (2019) document empirically for Chinese SOEs.

## Relevance for SAIC Motor paper

| Dimension | How to use |
|---|---|
| **Governance** | Shleifer & Vishny (1994) is the theoretical foundation for every governance claim in the seminar paper. SAIC's SASAC-controlled governance instantiates the "politician control" case: SASAC (the politician) has control rights over employment levels (e.g., SAIC employs ~200k workers — a major political asset for Shanghai), strategic location (Anting auto cluster), and JV structuring. Management does not have strong equity stakes. This is precisely the regime the model predicts will produce excess employment and capital misallocation. |
| **Financing** | Treasury transfers (T) in the model map to SAIC's preferential state bank access, SASAC approval of capital allocations, and state backing during crises. Under politician control, T is strategically calibrated — not to maximize firm value but to maintain political equilibrium. The −88% profit collapse in 2024 without governance restructuring is consistent with the model: politicians accept low T (high losses) rather than trigger restructuring. |
| **Capability Building** | Proposition 13 is particularly powerful: SASAC actively *resists* giving SAIC's management strong equity incentives because well-incentivized managers would resist political demands. This structural preference for weak management incentives is why SAIC cannot replicate BYD's founder-led, equity-incentivized governance — not just legacy but active political design. |
| **Long-term Viability** | The model predicts privatization/restructuring happens when B(L) falls and C(T) rises. In China's EV era: B(L) may be falling (EV-sector political prestige goes to BYD/private champions, not legacy SOE employment) and C(T) is rising (government fiscal position tightening). This creates conditions where the model predicts restructuring — but requires political will to transfer actual control rights, not just equity. |

## Relation to other sources

- **Founds** [[2026-05-27-fan-wong-zhang-2007-politically-connected-ceos-jfe]]: Fan et al. (2007) empirically test Shleifer & Vishny's predictions using Chinese IPO data. Their "grabbing hand" framing (p. 353) directly cites Shleifer & Vishny (1994). The theorized mechanism (politically connected managers extract resources for state objectives) is the empirical manifestation of Proposition 1–5.
- **Founds** [[2026-05-27-jia-huang-zhang-2019-public-governance-soe-innovation-amj]]: Jia et al.'s finding that SOEs optimize for measurable (quantity) rather than valuable (novel) innovation follows structurally from Shleifer & Vishny: politicians evaluate and reward visible outputs (patent counts, employment levels), not firm value. The multitasking moral hazard in Jia et al. is a special case of the general distortion in this model.
- **Founds** [[2026-05-27-bortolotti-faccio-2009-government-control-privatized-firms-rfs]]: Bortolotti & Faccio's central finding — that 62.4% of "privatized" firms remain government-controlled — is exactly what Proposition 1 predicts: without control rights transfer, privatization of cash flow rights is irrelevant and politicians retain influence. The civil-law pyramid (SASAC → SAIC Group → SAIC Motor) is a mechanism for retaining control while formally privatizing equity.
- **Contrasts with** [[byd-company]]: BYD's founder-led governance with Wang Chuanfu holding significant equity is the "manager control with strong ownership" case. Proposition 5 predicts this produces lower excess employment and more efficient resource allocation — consistent with BYD's superior EV execution.

## Notes

- **ABS 4*** — QJE (Quarterly Journal of Economics) is one of the top five economics journals. Alongside the Shleifer & Vishny (1998) *Journal of Political Economy* paper "The Grabbing Hand", this 1994 paper is the canonical theoretical foundation for SOE governance analysis.
