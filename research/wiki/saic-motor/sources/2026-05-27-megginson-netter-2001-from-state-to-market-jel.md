# From State to Market: A Survey of Empirical Studies on Privatization

**Megginson & Netter (2001) survey the empirical privatization literature and conclude: (1) privately owned firms are significantly more efficient and profitable than SOEs; (2) privatization consistently improves firm performance across profitability, efficiency, output, and investment; (3) China's SOEs are burdened with social welfare responsibilities that make full privatization politically difficult, but non-privatizing reforms alone likely leave efficiency gains on the table.**

**Full citation**: Megginson, W.L. and Netter, J.M. (2001) 'From state to market: A survey of empirical studies on privatization', *Journal of Economic Literature*, 39(2), pp. 321–389. JSTOR: 2698243.

**Journal**: Journal of Economic Literature — **ABS 4*** (premier economics survey journal; AEA publication)
**Affiliations**: William Megginson (University of Oklahoma); Jeffry Netter (University of Georgia)
**Method**: Systematic survey of empirical privatization literature; organizes evidence by methodology and region; synthesizes findings from ~100 studies
**Nature**: Authoritative literature review; establishes the empirical consensus on privatization effects

---

## Key takeaways

- **SOEs are less efficient than private firms**: Consensus across multiple methodologies — private firms earn higher returns, have lower leverage, are less labor-intensive, and grow productivity faster. The efficiency gap is 3–37% depending on country and industry (pp. 332–337, Section 3.3).
- **Privatization works**: Across 211 firms in 41 countries, privatized firms show significant post-privatization improvements: +3.96pp profitability, +19.14% efficiency (real sales per employee), +83.21% output, −4.69pp leverage, +2.28pp dividends. 67.6% of firms show improved profitability. These results are statistically significant at 1% in almost all cases (p. 354–356, Table 5).
- **Full privatization required**: Partial privatization — transferring cash flow rights but not control — produces little efficiency gain. Ehrlich et al. (1994) find the long-run productivity benefit requires *complete* ownership change; partial change has almost no effect (p. 334–335).
- **Soft budget constraint as SOE root cause**: SOEs face no credible bankruptcy threat, removing the market discipline that forces private firms to minimize costs and eliminate excess employment. "The state is unlikely to allow a large SOE to face bankruptcy" — cited as a major source of inefficiency (p. 331, Section 3.1.4).
- **China's unique position**: China has improved SOE performance through incentive contracts and deregulation without large-scale privatization. But the survey cautions: "there is limited empirical evidence... that suggests that non-privatizing reform measures... can improve the efficiency of SOEs, but it also seems likely that these reforms would be even more effective if coupled with privatization." (p. 381, Conclusion 2).
- **Chinese SOE social burden**: "Chinese SOEs are burdened with so many social welfare responsibilities [that] it will be extraordinarily difficult to implement a privatization program large enough to seriously undermine the state's economic role." (p. 325) — the employment and social function constraint is explicitly recognized as unique to China.
- **Mixed ownership (partial privatization) is not enough**: Boardman & Vining (1989) find that mixed enterprises (partial state/private ownership) are no more profitable than pure SOEs — **full private control** is required. Vining & Boardman (1992) find ownership has a separable effect from competition (p. 335, Table 1).
- **Competition matters too**: Several studies show competition and deregulation have independent positive effects on efficiency, and may be more important than ownership change in competitive markets (p. 330, 337).

## Claims (with journal page numbers)

- "Research now supports the proposition that privately owned firms are more efficient and more profitable than otherwise-comparable state-owned firms. There is limited empirical evidence, especially from China, that suggests that non-privatizing reform measures... can improve the efficiency of SOEs, but it also seems likely that these reforms would be even more effective if coupled with privatization." (p. 381, Conclusion 2)
- "We know that privatization 'works,' in the sense that divested firms almost always become more efficient, more profitable, and financially healthier, and increase their capital investment spending. These results hold for both transition and non-transition economies." (p. 381, Conclusion 5)
- "The state is unlikely to allow a large SOE to face bankruptcy. Thus the discipline enforced on private firms by capital markets and the threat of financial distress is less important for state-owned firms." (p. 331)
- "Ehrlich et al. (1994) find... that a partial change from state to private ownership has little effect on long-run productivity growth — the benefits are based on complete privatization of the firm." (p. 334–335)
- "SOEs and mixed (state and private) ownership enterprises are significantly less profitable and productive than are privately owned firms... mixed enterprises are no more profitable than SOEs, suggesting that full private control, not just partial ownership, is essential to achieving performance improvement." (p. 335, citing Boardman & Vining 1989)
- "[Chinese] SOEs are burdened with so many social welfare responsibilities suggests that it will be extraordinarily difficult to implement a privatization program large enough to seriously undermine the state's economic role." (p. 325)
- "[China] underwent widespread economic reform with minimal privatization through the use of [management] incentive contracts." (p. 337, citing Groves et al. 1995)
- "Government's inability to credibly commit to a policy can significantly reduce the efficiency of an SOE's operations and governance." (p. 330)
- "Privatization is a major change in the governance structure of a firm... how well the legal system protects investors is presumably a determinant of the success of privatization in improving firm performance." (p. 378)
- On employment: "privatization does not automatically mean employment reductions in divested firms — though this will likely occur unless sales can increase fast enough after divestiture to offset very large productivity gains." (p. 357)

## The MNR benchmark (Table 5 — 211 firms, 41 countries)

The survey's central empirical table aggregates results from three comparable studies using the same Megginson-Nash-van Randenborgh (1994) methodology:

| Performance variable | Before privatization | After privatization | Change | % firms improved | Significance |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Profitability** (net income/sales) | 8.62% | 12.57% | **+3.96pp** | 67.6% | 1% |
| **Efficiency** (real sales/employee) | 0.9733 | 1.1599 | **+19.14%** | 81.5% | 1% |
| **Investment** (capex/sales) | 14.05% | 19.00% | **+4.95pp** | 60.6% | 5% |
| **Output** (real sales) | 0.9358 | 1.7211 | **+83.21%** | 80.3% | 1% |
| **Leverage** (debt/assets) | 48.26% | 43.57% | **−4.69pp** | 67.0% | 1% |
| **Dividends** (dividends/sales) | 2.02% | 6.55% | **+2.28pp** | 80.4% | 1% |

*Source: p. 354–356, Table 5. Weighted average across MNR (1994), Boubakri & Cosset (1998), D'Souza & Megginson (1999).*

Note: Employment results are mixed (3 studies find increases, 5 find decreases) — no universal outcome.

## Theoretical mechanisms surveyed

The paper reviews six theoretical reasons why SOEs underperform (pp. 329–332):

1. **Market failure argument**: Privatization benefits are greatest where market failure is weakest (i.e., competitive markets). For natural monopolies, the case is less clear but state ownership still has weaknesses.
2. **Incomplete contracting**: Government cannot credibly commit to profit maximization as a goal; management contracts are harder to complete for public owners with diffuse/political principals.
3. **Transaction costs of intervention**: Government intervention costs less in public firms, so intervention is more frequent — even when it destroys value.
4. **Soft budget constraints** (Kornai 1988): No credible bankruptcy threat → no capital market discipline → no cost pressure. The most cited mechanism for chronic SOE inefficiency.
5. **Fiscal effects**: Privatization proceeds can reduce deficits; SOE subsidies constitute a fiscal drain. But fiscal effects are secondary to efficiency effects.
6. **Market development**: Privatization through SIPs develops capital markets, investor protection law, and governance institutions simultaneously.

## Relevance for SAIC Motor paper

| Dimension | How to use |
|---|---|
| **Governance** | The survey's consensus — that SOEs are significantly less efficient than private firms, and that partial privatization (mixed ownership) does not close this gap — provides the empirical baseline for SAIC's structural disadvantage vs. BYD. Boardman & Vining's finding that mixed enterprises are no more profitable than pure SOEs directly applies to SAIC's SSE-listed-but-SASAC-controlled structure. The survey also confirms that China's SOE governance has been modified via incentive contracts (Groves et al. 1995) — yet these reforms fall short of full efficiency gains. |
| **Financing** | The soft budget constraint section (p. 331) explains SAIC's continued government backing despite −88% profit in 2024. The survey frames this as a structural SOE property: "the state is unlikely to allow a large SOE to face bankruptcy." SAIC's access to state bank financing and SASAC capital injections are empirical manifestations of this mechanism. The cost is that SAIC lacks the capital-market discipline that forces BYD to make profitable technology bets. |
| **Capability Building** | The efficiency gap in the MNR table (+19.14% efficiency post-privatization) implies that SAIC's SOE status costs it roughly 15-20% in per-employee efficiency vs. what a fully privatized equivalent would achieve. Combined with Jia et al.'s innovation-quality problem and Howell's JV cannibalization, this is the quantitative scale of the governance drag. |
| **Long-term Viability** | The survey's China-specific note is particularly valuable: China's SOEs have improved via non-privatization reforms, but "these reforms would be even more effective if coupled with privatization." In the EV era, where the competitive gap with private-firm BYD is growing, the survey predicts SAIC's reform path (incentive contracts, governance codes) will remain insufficient without structural ownership change — which is politically blocked by the social welfare burden. |

## Relation to other sources

- **Empirical companion to** [[2026-05-27-shleifer-vishny-1994-politicians-and-firms-qje]]: Shleifer & Vishny (1994) provide the theoretical model; Megginson & Netter survey the empirical evidence that confirms it. The soft budget constraint and multiple-objectives problems the survey describes are the observable consequences of politician control.
- **Empirical baseline for** [[2026-05-27-fan-wong-zhang-2007-politically-connected-ceos-jfe]]: Fan et al. document the specific mechanism (politically connected CEOs underperform). Megginson & Netter establish the broader cross-country context: SOE underperformance is universal, not China-specific.
- **Contextualizes** [[2026-05-27-bortolotti-faccio-2009-government-control-privatized-firms-rfs]]: Bortolotti & Faccio explain *why* governments retain control (political incentives). Megginson & Netter document *what* this retention costs in efficiency terms.
- **Contrasts with** [[2026-05-27-howell-2018-jv-technology-adoption-backfired]]: Howell shows a specific JV-era cannibalization effect. Megginson & Netter provide the general SOE efficiency baseline — SAIC's problem pre-dates the JV model and would persist even without it.
- **References to** [[byd-company]]: BYD is the private-firm counterfactual. The +67.6% improvement rate in the MNR benchmark defines what fully private governance could theoretically deliver for a firm of SAIC's scale.

## Notes

- **ABS 4*** — Journal of Economic Literature is one of the AEA's three journals (alongside AER and JEP). It is the field's premier survey/review publication.
- The MNR table (Table 5) is probably the single most-cited empirical summary of privatization effects in the literature. Citing it in the seminar paper with the specific figures (+3.96pp profitability, +19.14% efficiency) from p. 354–356 provides strong quantitative grounding.
- The paper was written in 2001 — before the major wave of Chinese SOE partial listings. Its China observations are prescient: the social welfare burden constraint it identifies in 2001 is still the primary reason SAIC remains state-controlled in 2024.
- JSTOR stable URL: https://www.jstor.org/stable/2698243. No DOI assigned to 2001 JEL articles in the standard DOI system.
- The paper notes that China Telecom (a partial SIP) was already worth $102bn in market cap at time of writing (p. 374, Table 11) — showing that even partial Chinese SOE privatizations attract large valuations, but this does not change the governance analysis.
