# Knowledge, Bargaining Power, and the Instability of International Joint Ventures

**Inkpen & Beamish (1997) develop the standard theoretical framework for IJV instability: JVs become unstable when one partner acquires enough knowledge to eliminate its dependency on the other, shifting bargaining power and making the original JV bargain obsolete.**

**Full citation**: Inkpen, A.C., & Beamish, P.W. (1997). Knowledge, bargaining power, and the instability of international joint ventures. *Academy of Management Review*, 22(1), 177–202. https://doi.org/10.5465/amr.1997.9707180263

**Journal**: Academy of Management Review — ABS 4*, VHB A+
**PDF**: 27 pages (JSTOR 259228, pp. 177–202); theoretical paper, no primary data collection

---

## Key takeaways

- IJV instability is defined as "a major change in relationship status that was unplanned and premature from the perspective of either one or both partners" (p. 182) — not simply JV termination.
- The primary driver of instability is a **shift in partner bargaining power** caused by knowledge acquisition that eliminates a partner dependency.
- **Foreign partners** typically bring technology and production know-how; **local partners** contribute local market knowledge, regulatory access, and distribution networks. Both are sources of dependency — and both can erode.
- The **foreign partner's acquisition of local knowledge** is the more common path to instability: once the foreign partner understands the local market, it no longer needs the local partner and may convert the JV to a wholly owned subsidiary.
- The **local partner's acquisition of foreign technology** is harder (skills are tacit, partners can protect them), but if successful, it makes the foreign partner's contributions redundant — and the JV loses its rationale for the foreign side.
- **Knowledge parity = instability trigger**: when the knowledge gap between partners closes, the cooperative basis for the JV erodes.
- **Attachment** (accumulated history of partner interactions) is a moderating factor that can delay instability even after knowledge parity is reached (Prop. 7).

---

## Propositions

- **Proposition 1**: As the foreign partner's local knowledge increases → foreign partner's dependence on local partner decreases → bargaining power shifts → greater IJV instability likelihood (p. 188).
- **Proposition 2** (implied): As the local partner acquires the foreign partner's technology-based skills → foreign partner's unique contribution erodes → instability increases. But this is harder, because skills are more tacit and protectable than local market knowledge.
- **Proposition 7**: Changes in individual and structural attachment between IJV partners update the foreign partner's valuation of local knowledge — attachment can stabilize a JV even when knowledge parity is reached (p. 197).

## The 2×2 instability matrix (Figure 3, p. 188)

|  | Local partner: **Access** only | Local partner: **Acquires** foreign tech |
|---|---|---|
| **Foreign partner: Access** local knowledge only | Very stable — Cooperative | Moderately stable |
| **Foreign partner: Acquires** local knowledge | Moderately unstable | **Very unstable — Race to learn** |

The bottom-right "race to learn" quadrant is the most dangerous: both partners are actively extracting knowledge, and both will eventually eliminate their dependency.

## Claims

- Instability rates in international alliances are close to **50%** (Bleeke & Ernst 1991; Kogut 1988), cited at p. 177.
- The "race to learn" scenario (Hamel 1991): "the partner that learns the fastest dominates the relationship" — but this is not the only possible outcome; win-win stable JVs also exist (p. 178).
- In U.S.–China JVs (Yan & Gray 1994): "the overwhelming goal of Chinese firms in cooperating with Western firms was to learn the more advanced Western technology… [but] the Chinese partners did not significantly gain bargaining power through knowledge, because the U.S. partners protected their technologies" (p. 188).
- Foreign partners can maintain bargaining power by "making additional resource commitments to their technological capabilities" — continuous technology updating keeps the local partner dependent (p. 189).
- NUMMI (GM–Toyota): Toyota deployed knowledge acquired from NUMMI directly into its wholly-owned Georgetown, Kentucky plant — illustrating how local knowledge acquisition enables transition from JV to subsidiary (p. 189).
- Termination is not always mutual: one partner may view a still-functioning JV as unsuccessful because the balance of power has already shifted (p. 183).
- JV longevity ≠ JV success: a long-lived JV may reflect attachment and switching costs rather than genuine mutual benefit (p. 183).

## Application to SAIC

The Inkpen & Beamish framework maps directly onto SAIC's current situation:

**The SAIC case fits the bottom-right quadrant** ("very unstable — race to learn"):
- VW and GM have spent decades acquiring local market knowledge in China via SAIC; they understand Chinese consumer preferences, local suppliers, and regulatory pathways.
- SAIC, through the QPQ policy, has absorbed production quality capabilities from its JV partners — the quality gap between domestic and JV models narrowed from 65% to 33% between 2001 and 2014 ([[2026-06-03-bai-barwick-cao-li-2025-quid-pro-quo-aer]], p. 3).

**The knowledge parity trigger has been reached** in the EV era:
- China's domestic EV competency (BYD, NIO, Li Auto) has surpassed foreign OEM capabilities in battery technology and software-defined vehicles.
- VW and GM's core contribution — combustion engine and transmission technology — is becoming obsolete.
- Neither side is as dependent on the other as in 1984 or 1997.

**Observable consequence**: SAIC-GM sales fell −56.54% in 2024 — the structural result Inkpen & Beamish predict when knowledge parity eliminates the rationale for cooperation.

**Key tension**: Attachment (40 years of VW–SAIC relationship, legal JV contracts, sunk infrastructure) moderates the speed of instability (Prop. 7) but cannot indefinitely prevent it once the knowledge dependency is gone.
