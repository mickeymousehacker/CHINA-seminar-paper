# The Effects of Privatization and Ownership in Transition Economies

**Estrin et al. (2009) synthesize 30+ studies on privatization in postcommunist transition economies and China, establishing a clear ownership-performance hierarchy: foreign private ownership yields the largest gains, domestic private ownership produces smaller and variable gains, and insider/state ownership produces the weakest or negative results.**

**Full citation**: Estrin, S., Hanousek, J., Kočenda, E., & Svejnar, J. (2009). The effects of privatization and ownership in transition economies. *Journal of Economic Literature*, 47(3), 699–728. https://doi.org/10.1257/jel.47.3.699

**Journal**: Journal of Economic Literature — ABS 4*, VHB A+
**Method**: Structured meta-survey of 30+ enterprise-level studies across CEE (Central & Eastern Europe), Russia/CIS, and China; distinguishes foreign vs. domestic private ownership and controls for selection/endogeneity
**PDF**: 31 pages (JSTOR 27739983, pp. 699–728)

---

## Key takeaways

- **Ownership performance hierarchy** (from best to worst):
  1. **Foreign private ownership** → large, rapid, robust performance gains in TFP, profitability, labor productivity — "virtually everywhere in transition economies" (p. 723)
  2. **Concentrated domestic private ownership** → positive but quantitatively smaller than foreign; greater in later transition periods; positive in CEE, nil or negative in CIS
  3. **Dispersed/insider (employee/manager) ownership** → mostly insignificant or negative TFP effects; no reduction in excess employment
  4. **State ownership (SOE)** → worst outcome; innovation constraint, soft budget constraints, political objectives over profit

- **CEE vs. CIS divergence**: In CEE (Czech Republic, Hungary, Poland), domestic privatization has a delayed but positive effect — aligned with EU institutional reforms. In the CIS (Russia, Ukraine), domestic privatization to private owners is nil or even negative, because legal/institutional systems failed to constrain tunneling and looting.

- **China findings — mixed**: "The available papers on China find diverse results, with the effect of nonstate ownership on total factor productivity being mostly positive but sometimes insignificant or negative." (Abstract) — China's gradual reform process and larger institutional differences make results less clear-cut than CEE.

- **Why foreign > domestic**: Foreign owners bring (1) management capabilities and global networks, (2) access to capital markets, (3) advanced corporate governance systems that compensate for underdeveloped local legal institutions, and (4) systematic training of local managers (pp. 723–724). Domestic privatized firms frequently engaged in "looting, tunneling, and defrauding of minority shareholders" where legal institutions were weak.

- **Selection bias is critical**: A major methodological contribution — firms were not chosen for privatization randomly. Studies ignoring this endogeneity overstate privatization gains. The paper focuses on studies that address selection bias.

- **Complementary reforms matter**: Macro evidence shows that privatization accompanied by legal/institutional reforms (market-oriented legal system, property rights, contract enforcement) produces positive aggregate effects; without these, the gains are smaller or absent (p. 726).

---

## Claims

- Privatization to foreign owners produces "a fairly rapid shift in performance rather than a gradual improvement" (p. 723) — the gains arrive quickly once foreign owners take control.
- Privatization to domestic owners has "on average been less impressive and it has varied across regions": positive but delayed in CEE; nil or negative in CIS Russia (p. 723).
- "In China, the constraints imposed by the government… [affect] privatization effects" and results are more diverse — suggesting China's institutional context is distinct from both CEE and CIS (p. 724).
- De novo (greenfield) private firms tend to outperform domestically privatized SOEs in CEE and Russia, but not significantly so when selection bias is controlled (p. 718).
- The key mechanism behind foreign ownership gains: foreign owners "routinely bring in capable expatriate managers and invest heavily in training local managers. They sell products through their global distributional networks, introduce a relatively advanced system of corporate governance and stress the importance of business ethics." (p. 724)
- Private-sector share of GDP in transition economies rose from <10–30% in 1990 to 65–80% by mid-2000s in CEE (Table 1); this structural transformation is the empirical backdrop.
- TFP effect of domestic private ownership is larger in the **later** transition period (mid-to-late 1990s onwards) than the early period — institutions matter and take time to develop (Figure 1, p. 714).

## Application to SAIC

Estrin et al.'s ownership hierarchy maps directly onto SAIC's governance structure and explains why SAIC's performance lags BYD.

**SAIC's ownership type in the Estrin et al. framework:**

| Layer | Owner | Estrin et al. category | Expected performance |
|---|---|---|---|
| SAIC Motor itself | Shanghai SASAC (63.27% via SAIC Group) | **State ownership** | Worst outcome |
| SAIC-VW / SAIC-GM (JV level) | VW/GM (50%) + SAIC (50%) | Mixed: foreign + state | Intermediate |
| BYD (comparison) | Wang Chuanfu + institutional investors | Concentrated domestic private + founder-led | Better than SOE; still below foreign |

**The crucial implication**: Even if SAIC were fully privatized to domestic private owners, the Estrin et al. evidence suggests the gains would be "smaller, often delayed" — especially in China where TFP effects of nonstate ownership are "mostly positive but sometimes insignificant or negative." Full privatization to a **foreign** owner (e.g., VW or GM acquiring full SAIC-VW) would likely produce the largest efficiency gains.

**Why SAIC-VW/GM perform better than SAIC's own brands**: The JV structure means 50% foreign ownership at the JV level — exactly the "foreign ownership" category Estrin et al. identify as best-performing. SAIC's own brands (Roewe, MG) are 100% SAIC-controlled — the "domestic SOE" category. This explains why JV profitability has historically subsidized SAIC's underperforming own brands, and why the JV income collapse in 2024 is so structurally damaging.

**The China caveat**: Estrin et al. explicitly flag that China's gradual reform path produces more mixed results than the CEE/CIS transition pattern. China's party-state system, SASAC oversight, and political employment objectives create an institutional environment where even domestic privatization may not fully deliver efficiency gains — consistent with [[2026-05-27-jia-huang-zhang-2019-public-governance-soe-innovation-amj]]'s finding that quantity-over-novelty innovation bias persists under weak governance.
