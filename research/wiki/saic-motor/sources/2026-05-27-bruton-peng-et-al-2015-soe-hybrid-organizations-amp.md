# State-Owned Enterprises Around the World as Hybrid Organizations

**Bruton, Peng et al. (2015) argue that today's SOEs should not be treated dichotomously (state-owned or not) but as hybrid organizations on a continuum of ownership and control; using 36 cases from 23 countries they show that ownership and control are separable and that the soft budget constraint — not ownership level per se — is the primary driver of underperformance, with CEO background as a key micro-institutional moderator.**

**Full citation**: Bruton, G.D., Peng, M.W., Ahlstrom, D., Stan, C. and Xu, K. (2015) 'State-owned enterprises around the world as hybrid organizations', *Academy of Management Perspectives*, 29(1), pp. 92–114. https://doi.org/10.5465/amp.2013.0069

**Journal**: Academy of Management Perspectives — **ABS 3** (practitioner-facing Academy of Management journal; below AMJ/AMR at ABS 4*)
**Affiliations**: Bruton (Texas Christian University & Sun Yat-sen); Peng (UT Dallas); Ahlstrom (Chinese University of Hong Kong); Stan (Florida Atlantic); Xu (Sun Yat-sen)
**Method**: Purposive case analysis — 36 SOE cases from 23 countries across 4 industries (energy/natural resources, transportation, manufacturing, finance); 3×3 ownership × control matrix; supplemented by systematic review of FT45 SOE literature 2000–2014
**Nature**: Conceptual/empirical hybrid; framework-building paper with case evidence; practitioner-accessible

## Key takeaways

- **SOEs are not dichotomous**: Prior research treats firms as either state-owned or private. Bruton et al. document a 3×3 matrix of government ownership (>50% / 25–50% / <25%) × government control (high / medium / low) — nine types of hybrid SOE. Most real-world SOEs occupy the off-diagonal cells, where ownership and control are decoupled (pp. 100–101, Table 3).
- **Ownership ≠ control**: The most important finding: state control can be high even when ownership is low (via golden shares, appointment powers), and low even when ownership is high (Singapore's STATS ChipPAC: high ownership, private management, low control). China's SASAC exercises control through party mechanisms and appointment rights independently of formal equity percentage (p. 100).
- **SASAC explicitly cited as high-control instrument**: "Scholars report similar levels of control for SOEs in China, with the power to veto resting with the government or the controlling councils, such as the State-owned Assets Supervision and Administration Commission (SASAC) of the State Council." (p. 100)
- **Soft budget constraint as primary performance mechanism**: State support for loss-making firms eliminates market discipline, causes overinvestment, and causes firms to "ignore market signals and many key technologies, particularly during periods of technological ferment." (p. 102) — the EV era is precisely such a period.
- **CEO background as micro-institution**: SOEs with government-background CEOs in high-control environments focus on employment maintenance and social stability rather than efficiency or innovation. Private-background CEOs bring commercial skills and are better able to restructure and innovate (pp. 103–104). This maps directly to Fan et al.'s (2007) "grabbing hand" mechanism.
- **Institutional quality drives control, not ownership**: In countries with strong institutional support for private business (high economic freedom), government ownership tends to be decoupled from government control. In weak-institution countries (China, India, Russia), high ownership and high control cluster together — "cell 3" of the matrix (p. 103).
- **SOE literature is China-centric**: 30 of 39 FT-45 articles on SOEs from 2000–2014 were about Chinese SOEs (p. 105). The paper urges broader generalization to global SOE experience.
- **SOEs generate ~10% of global GDP**: SOEs represent ~20% of global equity market value and 9 of the 15 largest IPOs from 2005–2012 (p. 92–93, Table 1). The organizational form is not a relic — it is structurally persistent.

## Claims (with journal page numbers)

- "State-owned enterprises represent approximately 10% of global gross domestic product... represent approximately 20% of global equity market value." (p. 92, Abstract)
- "Firms have often been viewed dichotomously as either state owned or privately owned. Today, however, we encourage a more nuanced view of state-owned enterprises as hybrid organizations, in which the levels of ownership and control by the state can vary." (p. 92, Abstract)
- "In China, for example, control of the firm co[mes from the government through the CCP apparatus and appointment powers] rather than from equity ownership alone." (p. 97, partial text — ownership ≠ control for Chinese SOEs)
- "Scholars report similar levels of control for SOEs in China, with the power to veto resting with the government or the controlling councils, such as the State-owned Assets Supervision and Administration Commission (SASAC) of the State Council." (p. 100)
- On soft budget constraints: "state ownership is typically associated with soft budget constraints... the state will provide support to a firm with chronic losses, which softens budget constraints and virtually eliminates the possibility of its going out of business or of being properly restructured." (p. 102, citing Kornai 1992)
- "Easy money often causes firms to overinvest in production equipment and other physical assets, such that they wind up with overcapacity... They also tend to ignore market signals and many key technologies, particularly during periods of technological ferment." (p. 102)
- "Soft budget constraints can turn the focus of management away from the market or keep firms on a technological path that is becoming unviable." (p. 102)
- On CEO background: "CEOs of firms on the left side of the chart, where government control is low, almost always come from private industry. On the right side, where government control is high, we find it common that the executives come from the government, or have worked for SOEs consistently." (p. 103–104, Table 4)
- "The CEO who has worked for the state brings a mindset consistent with state goals and not necessarily with the profit-maximization goals associated with private firms. The focus of the government and of the CEO (who has a state background) tends to be on the maintenance of employment and other social concerns rather than necessarily firm efficiency." (p. 104)
- "Government control can negatively affect the SOE's economic performance or may push the SOE toward production maximization as opposed to innovation and ROI." (p. 104)
- On institutional determinants: "Government control in SOEs appears to be negatively correlated with the overall institutional development in support of private enterprise... three of the four cases [in cell 3: high ownership, high control] come from countries in the bottom third in terms of development of institutions to support private business (India, China, and Russia)." (p. 103)

## The 3×3 hybrid SOE matrix

| | **Low government control** | **Medium government control** | **High government control** |
|---|---|---|---|
| **High ownership (>50%)** | Cell 1: Ownership without control (e.g., Singapore STATS ChipPAC) | Cell 2 | Cell 3: Traditional SOE (e.g., China/India/Russia state enterprises; **SAIC Motor**) |
| **Medium ownership (25–50%)** | Cell 4 | Cell 5 | Cell 6: Control-heavy partial SOE |
| **Low ownership (<25%)** | Cell 7: "Privatized" with minimal state role | Cell 8 | Cell 9: Control via golden share (e.g., Brazilian Vale, Russian firms) |

*Source: pp. 100–101, Table 3. SAIC Motor placement (Cell 3) is this wiki's inference from ownership data.*

Key insight: **Cell 3 firms (traditional SOE, high ownership + high control) are the least market-responsive**. The paper finds cells 3 and 9 actually show high ROI in some cases — attributable to soft budget constraints giving a longer-term outlook, monopoly rents, or state subsidies (p. 102) — but the innovation and competitive dynamics literature suggests this advantage does not survive periods of technological disruption.

## Theoretical mechanisms

Three theoretical lenses applied to hybrid SOEs:

1. **Transaction cost economics** (Williamson): Hybrid SOEs combine market and hierarchy elements; the "visible hand" of SASAC direction operates alongside market competition for SAIC's products.

2. **Agency theory**: In SOEs, fiduciary duty runs to the organization/state, not to specific shareholders. This creates principal-principal conflicts (state vs. minority shareholders) and principal-agent conflicts (SASAC vs. managers) simultaneously. Neither set of conflicts is resolved by standard corporate governance mechanisms.

3. **Neoinstitutional theory**: Both macro-level institutions (country-level rules, legal system) and micro-level institutions (CEO mindset, management culture) shape SOE behavior. In high-control environments, a "mindset" develops among CEOs that prioritizes state goals over commercial performance — and this mindset persists even after formal governance reforms (pp. 98–99, 103–104).

## Relation to other sources

- **Operationalizes** [[2026-05-27-shleifer-vishny-1994-politicians-and-firms-qje]]: Shleifer & Vishny's politician-control model is the theoretical foundation; Bruton et al. provide a management-oriented taxonomy (the 3×3 matrix) that makes the same point for practitioners and business researchers.
- **Complements** [[2026-05-27-fan-wong-zhang-2007-politically-connected-ceos-jfe]]: Fan et al. document politically connected CEOs underperform empirically. Bruton et al. explain the micro-institutional mechanism: CEOs with government backgrounds bring a different mindset (employment/social goals > profit/innovation). The two papers together address *who* the CEOs are and *why their mindset differs*.
- **Complements** [[2026-05-27-bortolotti-faccio-2009-government-control-privatized-firms-rfs]]: Bortolotti & Faccio document cross-country ownership persistence. Bruton et al. add that control persists even when ownership doesn't — reinforcing that formal privatization is insufficient.
- **Complements** [[2026-05-27-megginson-netter-2001-from-state-to-market-jel]]: Megginson & Netter find partial privatization produces little improvement. Bruton et al. explain why structurally: cells with mixed ownership but high control (cells 6, 9) behave like traditional SOEs because control, not ownership, drives the behavioral difference.
- **Contextualizes** [[2026-05-23-saic-annual-report-2024]]: The Annual Report records SAIC's formal governance (SASAC → SAIC Group → SAIC Motor, board composition). Bruton et al. provide the framework to interpret this structure as "high ownership + high control = Cell 3 traditional SOE" — not a partially reformed hybrid.
