# Joint Ventures and Technology Adoption: A Chinese Industrial Policy That Backfired

**Howell (2018) shows that China's JV mandate created a "cannibalization effect": domestic firms with JVs reduced vehicle quality and price in response to fuel economy standards, while firms without JVs did not — JV rents discouraged innovation investment.**

**Full citation**: Howell, S.T. (2018). Joint ventures and technology adoption: A Chinese industrial policy that backfired. *Research Policy*, 47(8), 1448–1462. https://doi.org/10.1016/j.respol.2018.04.021

**Journal**: Research Policy — **ABS 3** (nicht A/A*, aber inhaltlich unverzichtbar als Kontrapunkt zu Bai et al. 2025)
**Affiliation**: NYU Stern
**Data**: Model-level sales data, Chinese auto market 1999–2013 (State Council Development Research Center)
**Method**: Difference-in-differences, using 2009 fuel economy standards as quasi-experiment

---

## Key takeaways

- JV rents created a **cannibalization effect**: the domestic firm earns profits from the foreign partner's sales — so upgrading its own technology would cannibalize those rents, reducing the incentive to innovate.
- In response to the 2009 fuel economy standards (which forced a quality-or-upgrade choice), **domestic firms with JVs reduced quality; domestic firms without JVs did not**.
- **Firms with JVs drove the negative effect**: SOEs and private firms without JVs were unaffected or less affected.
- **Exports**: since 2008, *firms without JVs* accounted for almost all Chinese passenger vehicle exports — firms with JVs did not export competitively.
- Opening quote from He Guangyan, Former Minister of Machinery & Industry (Reuters 2012): *"China's policy of requiring all foreign car makers to form local joint ventures is 'like opium' for Chinese firms and is failing to foster world-class indigenous automakers."* (p. 1448)

## Claims (with journal page numbers)

- "In response to fuel economy standards requiring firms to upgrade technology or sacrifice quality, firms with JVs reduced quality and price relative to their counterparts. Consistent with cannibalization, firms with JVs drive the negative effect." (p. 1448, Abstract)
- "The standards reduced torque in domestic models relative to foreign models by 11 percent. They reduced price by 15 percent, weight by 65 kg (about 5 percent of the mean), [and] power-to-weight by 6.5 percent." (p. 1457, Section 5.2, Table 2)
- "Since 2008, private firms and local SOEs without JVs have been responsible for almost all passenger vehicle exports." (p. 1457, Section 5.1)
- Competition amplifies the effect: firms with JVs in the same vehicle segment as their foreign partner showed -23% torque and price (p. 1457–1458, Table 5); SUV-specialized JV pairs showed -31% torque, -17% price, -150 kg weight (p. 1458, Table 6).
- "The cannibalization channel appears to outweigh any benefits from knowledge spillovers in the JV." (p. 1458, Section 5.3)
- "I present evidence that the JV mandate in China's auto sector is a distortionary barrier to technology adoption." (p. 1460, Section 7, Conclusion)
- "Beijing has consistently called for 'self-reliant Chinese car manufacturers who rank among the 500 largest global firms' (NDRC, 2004). Yet foreign brands dominate China's passenger vehicle market." (p. 1451, Section 2.1)
- Foreign firms incurred only **variable cost** to comply with fuel economy standards (technology already developed for Japan/Europe); domestic firms faced a **fixed cost** of new technology acquisition. (p. 1450, Section 2.2)

## Theoretical mechanism (Section 3)

Two competing forces:

| Force | Direction | Mechanism |
|-------|-----------|-----------|
| **Efficiency effect** (Gilbert & Newbery 1982) | ↑ innovation | JV partner reduces technology acquisition cost for domestic firm |
| **Cannibalization effect** (Arrow 1962 replacement effect) | ↓ innovation | JV rents make own innovation less attractive (would cannibalize foreign partner's sales) |

**Howell's finding**: Cannibalization dominates the efficiency effect. The more a domestic firm competes with its foreign JV partner in the same vehicle segment, the stronger the negative effect on innovation.

---

## Empirical design

- **Quasi-experiment**: China's 2009 fuel economy Phase 2 standards — stringent, binding, unanticipated in magnitude
- **Treatment**: domestic firms with JVs vs. domestic firms without JVs
- **Outcome**: vehicle quality proxies (torque, price, weight, power-to-weight ratio)
- **Period**: 1999–2013, ~426 models by 2012
- **Parallel trends**: confirmed pre-2009 (Appendix Table A6, p. 1459)

## Relation to other sources

- **Complements** [[2026-05-27-holmes-mcgrattan-prescott-2015-quid-pro-quo-res]]: Holmes et al. show QPQ transferred technology capital to China (welfare gain). Howell shows the transfer came with a hidden cost — innovation disincentive.
- **Complements** [[2026-05-23-saic-annual-report-2024]]: SAIC's 2024 collapse (net profit −88%) is the long-run outcome Howell's mechanism predicts.
- **Tension with** Bai et al. (2025): Bai finds positive spillovers (8.3% quality improvement). Howell finds negative innovation incentives. Both are true — spillovers occurred, but simultaneously, JV rents suppressed domestic R&D. The net effect on EV-era competitiveness was negative.
