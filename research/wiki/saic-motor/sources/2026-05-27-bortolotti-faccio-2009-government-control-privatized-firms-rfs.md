# Government Control of Privatized Firms

**Bortolotti & Faccio (2009) show that formal privatization does not mean actual loss of government control: 62.4% of a sample of OECD-privatized firms remained under government control (via ownership or golden shares) at end-2000; governments use two distinct instruments — direct pyramid ownership in civil-law systems, golden shares in common-law systems — and combined control is not meaningfully diminished by the choice of legal tradition.**

**Full citation**: Bortolotti, B. and Faccio, M. (2009) 'Government control of privatized firms', *Review of Financial Studies*, 22(8), pp. 2907–2939. https://doi.org/10.1093/rfs/hhn077

**Journal**: Review of Financial Studies — **ABS 4*** (top finance journal, alongside JFE and JF)
**Affiliations**: Bernardo Bortolotti (Università di Torino & FEEM); Mara Faccio (Purdue University, Krannert School of Management)
**Data**: 141 privatized firms from OECD countries (privatized before 1997); ownership tracked at end-1996 and end-2000; Stock market data from Datastream; Privatization Barometer and World Bank databases
**Method**: Cross-sectional analysis of ownership structure and control; ultimate-owner tracing through pyramids; probit regressions; firm-level, country-level determinants

## Key takeaways

- **Privatization ≠ loss of control**: 62.4% of firms classified as privatized (via IPO or trade sale before 1997) remained under government control at end-2000 — either through direct/ultimate ownership (≥20% threshold) OR through golden shares (p.2908, Abstract).
- **Government as largest owner**: Even at end-2000, the state was the largest ultimate shareholder in 29.79% of "privatized" firms (p.2914, Table 1) — a majority of the 50.74% of firms where a blockholder held ≥10%.
- **Golden shares are pervasive**: Golden shares were present in 62.5% of the sample (88 of 141 firms) (p.2919, Table 3). They grant the state appointment rights, veto power, and ownership constraints without requiring majority equity.
- **Civil law vs. common law split**: Civil-law countries (continental Europe, Japan) prefer direct/pyramid ownership; common-law countries (UK, Commonwealth) prefer golden shares. But combined government control intensity is **not significantly different** across legal traditions (p.2909, p.2923, Table 4 Panel C).
- **Ownership leverage**: Pyramid structures and dual-class shares give governments voting power far above cash-flow rights. Where the state is the largest shareholder, actual voting rights averaged 52.18% at end-2000 vs. an apparent 37.14% without leveraging — prior studies **systematically understated** government power (p.2932).
- **Political determinants dominate**: Proportional electoral systems and more centralized political authority significantly predict more government control post-privatization (p.2935, Section 4). Fiscal need (budget deficit) does not independently predict full privatization. Country political economy, not firm quality, drives government retention.
- **"Best-foot-forward" selection**: More profitable and valuable firms are privatized more completely. This means government retains control in weaker performers while selling stakes in stronger ones (p.2932, Table 5) — the opposite of what pure efficiency logic would prescribe.
- **Golden share powers**: Three categories: (1) right to appoint board members; (2) right to veto acquisitions, asset disposals, and changes of control; (3) constraints on other investors (e.g., caps on private blockholder stakes) (p.2918, Section 2.2).

- "Governments retain control of privatized firms through ownership and/or golden shares in 62.4% of our sample of privatized firms at the end of 2000." (p.2908, Abstract)
- "The government is the largest ultimate owner in 42 of the 82 privatized firms (51.2%) that have an ultimate owner with at least 10% of voting rights at end-2000." (p.2914, Table 1 panel)

- "Without the use of ownership-leveraging devices, the apparent equity stake owned by the government would be 37.14% versus the actual voting stake of 52.18%." (p.2932)
- "These results suggest that prior studies of privatization have substantially understated the degree of government power in privatized firms." (p.2932)
- Civil law / common law split: "Government control through ownership is more likely in civil law countries. Government control through golden shares is more likely in common law countries. The overall likelihood of government control... does not significantly differ across legal traditions." (p.2909, confirmed in Table 4, Panel C)
- "Our evidence suggests that political factors are particularly important determinants of the level of government control in privatized firms." (p.2908, Abstract)
- "Countries with proportional electoral systems and more centralized political authority retain more government control over privatized firms." (p.2935, Section 4 conclusion)
- On "best-foot-forward": "The government tends to retain control in the less valuable and less profitable firms." (p.2932, Table 5 discussion)
- Golden share appointment rights: "Several golden share contracts give the government the right to appoint a certain number of directors to the board of the privatized company." (p.2918, Section 2.2)
- Golden share veto powers: "The most common type of golden share gives the state the right to veto any change of control... as well as to veto the sale or other disposal of 'strategic' assets." (p.2918, Section 2.2)

## Theoretical framework

**The "incomplete privatization" problem**:

Standard privatization theory predicts that selling equity transfers residual claims and thus governance. Bortolotti & Faccio document systematic deviations: governments retain control because:

1. **Political economy of retention**: Elected governments use state-controlled firms to provide employment, implement industrial policy, and distribute rents to constituencies. Proportional electoral systems increase this incentive because coalition governments have more veto players requiring compensation.

2. **Two control instruments**:

| Instrument | Typical legal system | Mechanism |
|---|---|---|
| **Direct/pyramid ownership** | Civil law (France, Italy, Germany, Japan) | State holds majority stake directly or via state holding company; full voting control |
| **Golden shares** | Common law (UK, Commonwealth) | State holds special share giving appointment/veto rights; low equity cost |
| **Combined** | Either | Both simultaneously; combined control equivalent across traditions |

3. **Ownership leverage**: In pyramid structures (state → holding company → listed firm), the state's effective voting control consistently exceeds its cash-flow rights. Dual-class shares amplify this further. This is the same structural mechanism used by SAIC's ownership chain: Shanghai SASAC → SAIC Group → SAIC Motor.

4. **Selectivity**: Governments privatize the most profitable, visible assets (to maximize IPO proceeds / "best foot forward") while retaining control in strategic or weaker-performing firms. This inverts the prediction that privatization targets underperformers.

## Relevance for SAIC Motor paper

| Dimension | How to use |
|---|---|
| **Governance** | SAIC Motor is precisely the "incomplete privatization" case Bortolotti & Faccio document. SAIC Motor is listed on the SSE (600104) — formally a privatized public company — yet Shanghai SASAC retains ultimate control through a two-level pyramid: SASAC → SAIC Group (63.27%) → SAIC Motor. This is the civil-law direct-ownership pattern the paper identifies. The paper provides a cross-country baseline: SAIC is not exceptional — it is one of 62.4% of "privatized" firms globally that remain under government control. |
| **Financing** | Bortolotti & Faccio note that government-controlled privatized firms retain favorable access to state financing — the same capital access advantage noted in [[state-owned-enterprise]] and [[2026-05-23-saic-annual-report-2024]]. However, the "best-foot-forward" selection finding suggests that this access comes at the cost of strategic autonomy: the government captures gains in high performers and retains control in weaker ones. |
| **Capability Building** | The "incomplete privatization" framework explains why formal listing on SSE does not produce the governance discipline that investor pressure creates in fully private firms. Without a credible threat of hostile takeover or shareholder displacement of management (impossible when SASAC holds 63.27% via SAIC Group), the management incentive reforms that private-firm governance relies on cannot function. This is the structural root of what [[2026-05-27-fan-wong-zhang-2007-politically-connected-ceos-jfe]] and [[2026-05-27-jia-huang-zhang-2019-public-governance-soe-innovation-amj]] document at the firm level. |
| **Long-term Viability** | The political determinants of government retention (proportional systems, centralized authority) map well to China's political structure. Bortolotti & Faccio's finding that political factors dominate firm-level factors in determining retention implies that SAIC's government control is not contingent on SAIC's performance — it is structurally stable as long as China's political system remains. This limits the likelihood of governance reform even as EV-era competitive pressure mounts. |

## Relation to other sources

- **Extends** [[2026-05-27-fan-wong-zhang-2007-politically-connected-ceos-jfe]]: Fan et al. show *consequences* of government control (politically connected CEOs underperform). Bortolotti & Faccio provide the *mechanism* explaining why government control persists despite poor performance — political incentives override efficiency. Together: structure persists (Bortolotti & Faccio) → bureaucrats appointed (Fan et al.) → firm underperforms (Fan et al.) → government retains control anyway (Bortolotti & Faccio).
- **Contextualizes** [[2026-05-27-jia-huang-zhang-2019-public-governance-soe-innovation-amj]]: Jia et al. study Chinese SOE innovation under state control. Bortolotti & Faccio show this is a general cross-country pattern, not China-specific pathology — governments globally retain post-privatization control, and the resulting governance deficits are structural, not accidental.
- **Contextualizes** [[state-owned-enterprise]]: Adds cross-country comparative baseline. SAIC's pyramid ownership (SASAC → SAIC Group → SAIC Motor) is the same civil-law pyramid pattern documented across OECD privatized firms.
- **Complements** [[2026-05-23-saic-annual-report-2024]]: The Annual Report records SAIC Group holding 63.27% of SAIC Motor — exactly the kind of pyramid ownership Bortolotti & Faccio identify as the civil-law instrument of government control without formal golden shares.
- **Contrasts with** [[byd-company]]: BYD's founder Wang Chuanfu and institutional investors (Berkshire Hathaway) hold controlling stakes without government ownership — the "fully privatized" end of the spectrum Bortolotti & Faccio would classify as genuinely private.
