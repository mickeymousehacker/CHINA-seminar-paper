# Quid Pro Quo: Technology Capital Transfers for Market Access in China

**Holmes, McGrattan & Prescott quantify the welfare effects of China's quid-pro-quo policy using a multicountry dynamic general equilibrium model and patent microdata, finding China gains ~5% in consumption while advanced economies lose 0.3–0.5%.**

**Full citation**: Holmes, T.J., McGrattan, E.R., & Prescott, E.C. (2015). Quid pro quo: Technology capital transfers for market access in China. *Review of Economic Studies*, 82(3), 1154–1193. https://doi.org/10.1093/restud/rdv008

**Journal**: Review of Economic Studies — ABS 4*, VHB A+
**PDF**: 40 pages (journal pp. 1154–1193); downloaded from Oxford Academic

---

## Key takeaways

- China's QPQ policy generates a **~5% welfare gain for China** (in consumption terms) and a **0.3–0.5% welfare loss for advanced economies** (US, Western Europe, Japan) over 1990–2010.
- QPQ functions as a **tax on technology capital**: foreign firms hold back on how much technology they deploy in China because any technology transferred becomes Chinese property inside China.
- **Patent evidence** shows the property rights transferred via JVs apply only inside China: jointly owned Chinese patents by foreign+domestic partners do not link to patent applications in other countries. Only the foreign partner's name appears on international applications — the Chinese partner's rights stop at the border.
- The automobile industry is the primary sector: 1994 Chinese automotive policy required JVs to have "the capacity for manufacturing products which attain the international technological levels of the 1990s" (p. 1161).
- Even after China's WTO accession (2001), QPQ continued as **implicit policy** via JV requirements: 85% of US–China Business Council survey respondents (2012) confirmed technology transfer requirements were at least somewhat present (p. 1161–1162).

## Claims (with journal page numbers)

- "By the 1970s, quid pro quo policy, which requires multinational firms to transfer technology in return for market access, had become a common practice in many developing countries." (p. 1154 / Abstract)
- "The fact that quid pro quo acts like a tax induces foreign firms to hold back on the level of technology capital they deploy in a host country." (p. 1155)
- "The advanced economies lose the equivalent of about 0.3–0.5% in consumption because of China's quid pro quo... China benefits significantly from the policy, with a gain in consumption of nearly 5%." (p. 1156)
- "China benefits from quid pro quo so much that it would choose to follow this policy even if other countries choose to follow it as well. In contrast, the U.S. and Western Europe are left worse off in a world in which all countries follow quid pro quo." (p. 1156)
- "In 1994, China's State Planning Commission introduced a policy initiative that detailed the specific technology transfer requirements for foreign firms that wanted to enter the domestic automobile market. All foreign investment had to be in the form of a joint venture with a domestic partner." (p. 1161)
- "In the 2007 document, under item 19, 'manufacturing of complete automobiles', is the stipulation that 'foreign investment shall not exceed 50 percent'." (p. 1162)
- Patent finding: "jointly owned Chinese patents tend not to link to patent applications in foreign countries." In the Alcatel-Lucent / Shanghai Bell example: "Shanghai Bell ownership applies only in China, whereas Alcatel-Lucent ownership applies in China and everywhere else." (p. 1158)
- "[Shanghai GM] can sell Chevrolets and Buicks in China, but not in the U.S." — property rights are China-specific. (p. 1155)

## Model structure

- **Six economies**: China, US, Western Europe, Japan, BRI (Brazil/Russia/India), Rest of World
- **Key concept**: "technology capital" = non-rivalrous accumulated know-how (R&D, brands) deployable across locations
- **Counterfactual**: China abandons QPQ starting in 1991 → less technology transfer → less domestic capability accumulation → lower Chinese welfare
- **Data**: Chinese patent records (State Intellectual Property Office of China, 1985–2010, 3.6M patents), FDI flow data 1990–2010

## Relevance for SAIC Motor paper

| Dimension | How to use |
|-----------|-----------|
| **Governance** | The 1994 automotive policy (p. 1161) is the regulatory foundation for SAIC's 50/50 JV structure. QPQ was state-enforced governance design, not a voluntary arrangement. |
| **Financing** | The welfare analysis explains why VW and GM accepted the JV deal despite technology losses: market access to China was worth it. QPQ as "implicit capital" contribution by the Chinese state. |
| **Capability Building** | Technology capital transferred via JVs applies only inside China (patent evidence, pp. 1155–1158). SAIC could not exploit this technology outside China — it built domestic, not global, competitiveness. |
| **Long-term Viability** | "Foreign firms may have stronger incentives to introduce advanced technologies into China when they have majority stakes" (p. 1156 / conclusion hint). Now that the 50% cap is lifted, this incentive structure changes — and the welfare balance shifts. |
