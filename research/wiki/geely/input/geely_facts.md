## Corporate Structure

- Zhejiang Geely Holding Group (ZGH) is a private company 100% owned by Li Shufu (Eric Li).
- ZGH sits above the publicly listed Geely Automobile Holdings (HKEX: 0175; OTC: GELYY/GELYF).
- ZGH + Proper Glory Holding Inc. together control ~76% of listed Geely Auto; single-class shares (one share, one vote).
- The holding structure uses family trusts and holding vehicles to maintain control continuity.
- ZGH board nominates directors to the listed Geely Auto board.

## The Volvo Cars Acquisition (2010)

- **Seller:** Ford Motor Company
- **Price:** USD 1.8bn for 100%
- **Announced:** March 28, 2010; **Completed:** August 2, 2010
- **What transferred:** Nine product series, three platforms, 2,400+ global dealer networks, brand, talent, supplier relationships
- **Deal structure:** USD 200m seller note from Ford; remainder cash
- **Assumed debt:** ~USD 3.5bn existing Volvo debt absorbed by Geely
- **Total capital raised:** ~USD 2.7bn (USD 1.8bn purchase + USD 900m working capital)
- **Financing mix:** Bank of China, China Construction Bank, Export-Import Bank of China, listed Geely Auto equity, Gothenburg city government, ~USD 932m Chinese central government (~50% of total)
- **Leverage impact:** ZGH group debt rose from RMB 16.05bn (2009) to RMB 71.07bn (2010); debt/capital ratio hit 73.47%

## Post-Acquisition Integration — Four-Stage Model

Source: Zheng, Q., Noorderhaven, N., & Du, J. (2022). *Journal of World Business, 57*(3). DOI: 10.1016/j.jwb.2021.101305
⚠️ Previously misattributed to Alvstam & Ivarsson (2020) — now corrected.

1. **Distancing** — Volvo operates as a standalone; brand and engineering autonomy fully preserved
2. **Balancing** — Selective resource sharing begins; management integration starts
3. **Building** — Active joint capability development; co-investment in engineering; CMA platform co-developed
4. **Diversifying** — Shared platform deployed across both brand families; enables Polestar, Lynk & Co

Zheng et al. (2022) argue that external legitimacy pressures (host-country perception) and internal legitimacy pressures (workforce trust) drive the sequencing of these stages — not purely operational logic.

Alvstam & Ivarsson (2020) [Chapter 8 in McKelvey & Jin (Eds.), Edward Elgar, pp. 129–143] independently document the same case but focus on the specific technology transfer mechanisms: greenfield plants, engine plant collaboration, supply-system coordination, joint R&D, and the CMA platform. The four-stage framing is Zheng et al.'s contribution.

- Volvo Cars profitable by the second year post-acquisition
- Geely Auto sales +134.5% within seven years of deal
- Geely R&D spend +600% within seven years of deal
- Patent convergence between Geely and Volvo confirmed by AJTI (2022), DOI 10.1080/19761597.2022.2133777
- CMA (Compact Module Architecture) is the flagship co-development output

## Key Academic Framing

- Geely described as an "emerging market acquirer" / "latecomer" using cross-border M&A to close capability gaps rapidly (vs. organic growth or passive JV dependency)
- Strategy characterised as "strategic asset-seeking FDI" — acquiring technology, brands, and know-how rather than markets or cheap labour
- Yakob, Nakamura & Ström (2018) in *Technovation* (Vol. 70–71, pp. 59–72) frame it as "strategic asset-creation and innovation upgrading"
