## Section 1.2 — Introduction paragraph (Geely as M&A-driven private model)

Geely's rise as a global automotive conglomerate exemplifies a distinctly private, acquisition-driven governance model, architected by its founder and chairman Li Shufu, who retains sole ownership of the apex holding company, Zhejiang Geely Holding Group (ZGH) (Alvstam & Ivarsson, 2014). The decisive inflection point came in 2010, when ZGH acquired Volvo Cars from Ford Motor Company for USD 1.5 billion — a transaction widely regarded as contrarian at the time, given Volvo's distressed financial position — thereby securing not only a premium Western brand but access to advanced platforms, supplier networks, and engineering talent that Geely could not have developed organically at comparable speed (Balcet et al., 2012). Rather than absorbing Volvo into the parent entity, Li Shufu pursued a deliberate arm's-length integration philosophy — encapsulated in the "run Volvo as Volvo" stance — that academic research identifies as instrumental in preserving Volvo's innovation capacity while enabling technology spillovers, most notably through the jointly developed CMA (Compact Module Architecture) platform (Zheng, Noorderhaven & Du, 2022; Alvstam & Ivarsson, 2020). The Volvo deal established an M&A template that ZGH replicated across subsequent transactions: the acquisition of the London Electric Vehicle Company (LEVC) in 2013, a 51% controlling stake in British sports car brand Lotus in May 2017 (Meng & Wang, 2021), and the formation of a 50:50 joint venture with Mercedes-Benz for the Smart brand in January 2020 with registered capital of RMB 5.4 billion. Taken together, these moves constitute a systematic strategy of acquiring strategic assets — brands, platforms, and technology — through capital markets access and selective state financing, rather than through vertical integration or state-directed joint ventures.

## Section 2.3 — Geely: M&A-driven Private Governance

Geely's governance model represents a third distinct archetype in Chinese automotive development: a privately held conglomerate that deploys cross-border M&A as its primary capability-building mechanism. Zhejiang Geely Holding Group (ZGH) is wholly owned by founder and chairman Li Shufu; his aggregate attributable interest in the publicly listed Geely Automobile Holdings (HK: 0175) is held through Geely Holding (39.20%) and Proper Glory Holding Inc. (25.24%), together approximately 41% of issued share capital as at 31 December 2024, with Li Shufu retaining sole private ownership of the unlisted ZGH apex entity (Geely Automobile Holdings, 2025a, pp. 76, 81). This concentrated founder-ownership structure — unencumbered by state committee approval processes or joint-venture partner consent — confers the strategic agility to execute rapid, large-scale foreign acquisitions (Alvstam & Ivarsson, 2014).

Luo and Tung's (2007) springboard perspective provides the appropriate theoretical anchor: emerging-market firms use cross-border acquisitions of developed-market assets as springboards to overcome latecomer disadvantages in technology, brands, and management competence. ZGH has operationalised this logic across a sequenced portfolio of transactions: the USD 1.5 billion acquisition of Volvo Cars from Ford Motor Company in 2010, the acquisition of LEVC (London Electric Vehicle Company) in 2013, a 51% controlling stake in Lotus Cars in May 2017 (Meng & Wang, 2021), and the formation of a 50:50 joint venture with Mercedes-Benz for the Smart brand in January 2020, with registered capital of RMB 5.4 billion.

The governance challenge of administering this multi-brand portfolio is resolved through differentiated integration rather than centralised control. Zheng, Noorderhaven and Du (2022, *Journal of World Business*, DOI: 10.1016/j.jwb.2021.101305) document how Geely managed the Volvo relationship through four sequential stages — distancing, balancing, building, and diversifying — driven by legitimacy pressures from both host-country stakeholders and acquired-firm employees. This arm's-length philosophy preserved Volvo's engineering culture while enabling capability transfer, most concretely through the jointly developed Compact Module Architecture (CMA) platform (Alvstam & Ivarsson, 2020); analogous logic grants Lotus its British performance identity and the Smart JV formally co-equal board representation.

By contrast, SAIC Motor's state-directed governance constrains strategic autonomy through its SOE ownership structure, while BYD's founder-led model pursues capability through a vertically integrated model including battery and semiconductor production. Geely's approach trades the cost efficiency of vertical ownership for the speed of strategic asset acquisition and the international legitimacy conferred by established Western brands.

## Section 3.3 — Geely: Acquisition Financing

The Volvo acquisition of 2010 represents the paradigm case of leveraged, state-assisted cross-border acquisition financing in Chinese automotive history. Balcet, Wang and Richet (2012, *IJATM*) document the headline deal structure: ZGH paid USD 1.5 billion for 100% of Volvo Cars — including a USD 200 million financing contribution from Ford, with the balance drawn from a three-party coalition of Chinese entities that collectively provided USD 1.1 billion (Beijing Geely Kaisheng International Investment Co., Daqing State Owned Assets Co., and Shanghai Jiaerwo Co.), and a further USD 200 million from China Construction Bank (London). The broader financing coalition has been reported in contemporaneous accounts to have included additional loans from Chinese state banks and contributions from the city of Gothenburg.

The strategic logic was speed. Organic development of equivalent brand, platform, and market assets would have required decades; the acquisition compressed that timeline to a single transaction. The thesis was vindicated by Volvo Cars' IPO on Nasdaq Stockholm in October 2021, which valued the company at approximately USD 18.5 billion — more than ten times the 2010 acquisition price — while ZGH retained a ~75–80% ownership stake, realising substantial unrealised gains.

Geely's subsequent portfolio carries material refinancing risk, with several subsidiaries including Polestar and Lotus Technology recording significant losses, creating contingent liabilities at ZGH group level (Geely Automobile Holdings, 2025a).

The contrast to peer financing models is instructive. BYD's vertically integrated model builds capability organically, avoiding acquisition debt but constraining the speed and geographic breadth of expansion. SAIC Motor's joint venture model shares capital requirements with foreign partners, limiting balance-sheet exposure while also constraining strategic independence. Geely's leverage-for-speed approach carries the highest financing risk of the three models, with group-level debt-servicing capacity remaining structurally dependent on Volvo's sustained profitability.

## Section 4.3 — Geely: Acquisition-based Capability Building

The 2010 Volvo acquisition gave ZGH instantaneous access to a cluster of strategic assets that would have taken Geely comparable decades to develop organically: Volvo's safety engineering expertise, a portfolio of vehicle platforms, accumulated brand equity, and an extensive global distribution network. In the academic literature this is characterised as strategic asset-seeking FDI — Geely did not acquire Volvo for Swedish market access but for the engineering and institutional capabilities embedded in the brand, capabilities that then served as the basis for upgrading Geely's own products and processes (Yakob et al., 2018).

The mechanism of absorption is documented in the four-stage integration model proposed by Zheng, Noorderhaven and Du (2022, *Journal of World Business*, DOI: 10.1016/j.jwb.2021.101305): distancing, balancing, building, and diversifying. The flagship output of the building stage is the jointly developed Compact Module Architecture (CMA) platform, deployed across Volvo's 40-series models, the Lynk & Co brand, and Geely-branded vehicles — concrete evidence that the transfer evolved from one-way absorption into bilateral co-development (Alvstam & Ivarsson, 2020). That the deal catalysed rather than substituted for internal investment is evidenced by a substantial increase in Geely's R&D expenditure in the years following the acquisition — a trajectory that continued into the EV era: in H1 2025, Geely Automobile Holdings reported R&D expenditure of RMB 7.33 billion (+21% year-on-year), equivalent to approximately 4.9% of H1 2025 revenue, confirming that external acquisition capability and internal innovation spend are complements rather than substitutes in Geely's model (Geely Automobile Holdings, 2025b).

Konda, Slepnikov and Jin (2022, *Asian Journal of Technology Innovation, 31*(3), DOI: 10.1080/19761597.2022.2133777) supply the quantitative foundation: longitudinal patent portfolio analysis reveals measurable convergence in the innovation outputs of Geely and Volvo over the post-acquisition period, distinguishing Geely-Volvo as a rare case of genuine co-creation among emerging-market acquirers rather than a one-directional extraction of developed-market knowledge.

The contrast with BYD's capability-building model is structurally significant. BYD's vertically integrated model — including battery and semiconductor production — follows a slower organic trajectory, but confers full proprietary control with no integration risk. Geely's acquisition route compressed the capability timeline decisively, yet its success remained contingent on sustaining the arm's-length governance conditions that protected Volvo's engineering culture; any deterioration of that relationship would have undermined the very assets the acquisition was designed to absorb.

## Section 5.3 — Geely: Long-term Viability

*(Updated with H1 2025 interim results — HKEX filing, 14 August 2025)*

Geely's FY2024 core performance was strong: Geely Automobile Holdings reported revenue of CNY 240.2 billion (+34% year-on-year) and net profit of RMB 16.8 billion (+240%) (Geely Automobile Holdings, 2025a).

In H1 2025, Geely Automobile Holdings reported revenue of RMB 150.3 billion (+27% year-on-year), with total vehicle sales of 1,409,180 units (+47%). NEV sales reached 725,151 units (+126%), representing 51.5% of total volume — the first half-year in which NEVs constituted a majority of Geely's sales mix. Adjusted operating profit for H1 2025 was RMB 6.66 billion (+102% year-on-year). The gearing ratio rose to 22.2% (from 8.8% at end-2024), driven by the RMB 9.1 billion ZEEKR acquisition of Lynk & Co (completed February 2025). Net cash of RMB 38.9 billion and operating cash flow of RMB 15.0 billion confirm solvency headroom. The group raised its full-year 2025 sales target to 3.0 million units in July 2025 (Geely Automobile Holdings, 2025b).

The EV transition introduces a strategic dependency structurally absent from BYD's model: Geely's electrification relies on platforms developed through Volvo Cars and the Smart JV — Volvo Cars a separately listed entity, the Smart JV a privately held 50:50 arrangement with Mercedes-Benz, neither of which ZGH controls unilaterally — rather than on vertically integrated battery production and EV platforms developed in-house. The NEV majority milestone, however, demonstrates that this platform-dependency has not constrained competitive pace: a 51.5% NEV share in H1 2025 positions Geely Automobile Holdings ahead of most global OEMs, if still behind BYD's near-total electrification (Geely Automobile Holdings, 2025b).

The overall assessment is asymmetric, but has shifted from neutral to cautiously positive on the near-term vector. A high-performing core in Geely Auto and Volvo Cars sustains the group; the NEV majority milestone and H1 2025 adjusted profit growth of +102% indicate that the transition is executing on schedule (Geely Automobile Holdings, 2025b). The surrounding portfolio of loss-making subsidiaries — Polestar, Lotus Technology — creates contingent liabilities that, if crystallised simultaneously, would materially strain ZGH's leveraged balance sheet and test the resilience of the acquisition-led model that generated the core strength in the first place (Geely Automobile Holdings, 2025a; Geely Automobile Holdings, 2025b). That risk has not disappeared, but the H1 2025 results demonstrate that the core is generating sufficient cash to absorb it for now.

## Conclusion — Which Governance Model is Most Likely to Succeed in the Long Run?

The three governance models examined in this paper each embody a coherent logic suited to the conditions in which it emerged. SAIC Motor's state-directed joint venture model offered an optimal response to the technology access and market constraints of China's pre- and early-post-WTO industrial environment. BYD's vertically integrated founder-led model pursued capability through proprietary ownership, constructing a self-sufficient supply chain spanning lithium-iron-phosphate cells, silicon carbide semiconductors, and complete vehicle platforms. Geely's M&A-driven private model traded the cost efficiency of vertical ownership for the speed of strategic asset acquisition and the international legitimacy conferred by established Western brands — compressing into a single transaction the accumulated latecomer disadvantages that Luo and Tung (2007) identify as the defining constraint on emerging-market firms in global competition.

Of the three, BYD's vertically integrated model is most likely to succeed in the long run. Battery cost constitutes the single largest determinant of EV profitability, and vertical integration over cell chemistry, manufacturing, and pack design confers a durable cost floor that neither JV-dependent nor acquisition-dependent rivals can easily replicate. Where SAIC's electrification strategy depends on co-developed platforms shared with foreign JV partners, and where Geely's relies on platforms controlled through Volvo Cars and the Smart JV — neither of which ZGH controls unilaterally — BYD's electrification architecture is self-contained and wholly proprietary.

SAIC Motor's JV model faces the most acute structural headwinds. The joint venture framework was designed for a world in which technology originated in Western OEMs and market access was controlled by the Chinese state — precisely the conditions that are now reversing. Geopolitical tensions and reciprocal tariff regimes have raised the cost of the foreign partnerships on which SAIC's product portfolio depends.

Geely occupies an intermediate position. Its acquisition-led approach has clearly succeeded on the capability-building and financial returns dimensions: the Volvo Cars IPO on Nasdaq Stockholm in October 2021, against a USD 1.5 billion acquisition price (Balcet et al., 2012), valuing the asset at approximately USD 18.5 billion by 2021, and the NEV majority milestone of H1 2025, both confirm that the model can deliver competitive results at scale. Yet the contingent liabilities embedded in the portfolio — Polestar's going-concern qualification and Lotus Technology's accumulated losses — represent a correlated tail risk structurally absent from BYD's model.

The broader implication is that the era in which governance model choice could be separated from technological trajectory is closing. The vertically integrated model is structurally aligned with the competitive logic of electrification — proprietary control over the cost-defining component — and is therefore best positioned to generate durable returns as the industry completes its transition.
