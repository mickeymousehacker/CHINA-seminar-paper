## Section 1.2 — Introduction paragraph (Geely as M&A-driven private model)

Geely's rise as a global automotive conglomerate exemplifies a distinctly private, acquisition-driven governance model, architected by its founder and chairman Li Shufu, who retains sole ownership of the apex holding company, Zhejiang Geely Holding Group (ZGH). The decisive inflection point came in 2010, when ZGH acquired Volvo Cars from Ford Motor Company for USD 1.5 billion — a transaction widely regarded as contrarian at the time, given Volvo's distressed financial position — thereby securing not only a premium Western brand but access to advanced platforms, supplier networks, and engineering talent that Geely could not have developed organically at comparable speed (Balcet et al., 2012). Rather than absorbing Volvo into the parent entity, Li Shufu pursued a deliberate arm's-length integration philosophy — encapsulated in the "run Volvo as Volvo" stance — that academic research identifies as instrumental in preserving Volvo's innovation capacity while enabling technology spillovers, most notably through the jointly developed CMA (Compact Module Architecture) platform (Zheng, Noorderhaven & Du, 2022). The Volvo deal established an M&A template that ZGH replicated across subsequent transactions: the acquisition of the London Electric Vehicle Company (LEVC) in 2013, a 51% controlling stake in British sports car brand Lotus in May 2017, and the formation of a 50:50 joint venture with Mercedes-Benz for the Smart brand in January 2020 with registered capital of RMB 5.4 billion. Taken together, these moves constitute a systematic strategy of acquiring strategic assets — brands, platforms, and technology — through capital markets access and selective state financing, rather than through vertical integration or state-directed joint ventures.

## Section 2.3 — Geely: M&A-driven Private Governance

Geely's governance model represents a third distinct archetype in Chinese automotive development: a privately held conglomerate that deploys cross-border M&A as its primary capability-building mechanism. Zhejiang Geely Holding Group (ZGH) is wholly owned by founder and chairman Li Shufu; his aggregate attributable interest in the publicly listed Geely Automobile Holdings (HK: 0175) is held through Geely Holding (39.20%) and Proper Glory Holding Inc. (25.24%), together approximately 41% of issued share capital as at 31 December 2024, with Li Shufu retaining sole private ownership of the unlisted ZGH apex entity (Geely Automobile Holdings, 2025a, p. 76). This concentrated founder-ownership structure — unencumbered by state committee approval processes or joint-venture partner consent — confers the strategic agility to execute rapid, large-scale foreign acquisitions (Alvstam & Ivarsson, 2014).

Luo and Tung's (2007) springboard perspective provides the appropriate theoretical anchor: emerging-market firms use cross-border acquisitions of developed-market assets as springboards to overcome latecomer disadvantages in technology, brands, and management competence. ZGH has operationalised this logic across a sequenced portfolio of transactions: the USD 1.5 billion acquisition of Volvo Cars from Ford Motor Company in 2010, the acquisition of LEVC (London Electric Vehicle Company) in 2013, a 51% controlling stake in Lotus Cars in May 2017, and the formation of a 50:50 joint venture with Mercedes-Benz for the Smart brand in January 2020, with registered capital of RMB 5.4 billion (Balcet et al., 2012).

The governance challenge of administering this multi-brand portfolio is resolved through differentiated integration rather than centralised control. Zheng, Noorderhaven and Du (2022, *Journal of World Business*, DOI: 10.1016/j.jwb.2021.101305) document how Geely managed the Volvo relationship through four sequential stages — distancing, balancing, building, and diversifying — driven by legitimacy pressures from both host-country stakeholders and acquired-firm employees. This arm's-length philosophy preserved Volvo's engineering culture while enabling capability transfer, most concretely through the jointly developed Compact Module Architecture (CMA) platform (Alvstam & Ivarsson, 2020); analogous logic grants Lotus its British performance identity and the Smart JV formally co-equal board representation.

By contrast, SAIC Motor's state-directed governance constrains strategic autonomy through its SOE ownership structure, while BYD's founder-led model pursues capability through a vertically integrated model including battery and semiconductor production. Geely's approach trades the cost efficiency of vertical ownership for the speed of strategic asset acquisition and the international legitimacy conferred by established Western brands.

## Section 3.3 — Geely: Acquisition Financing

The Volvo acquisition of 2010 represents the paradigm case of leveraged, state-assisted cross-border acquisition financing in Chinese automotive history. Balcet, Wang and Richet (2012, *IJATM*) document the headline deal structure: ZGH paid USD 1.5 billion for 100% of Volvo Cars — including a USD 200 million financing contribution from Ford, with the balance drawn from a three-party coalition of Chinese entities that collectively provided USD 1.1 billion (Beijing Geely Kaisheng International Investment Co., Daqing State Owned Assets Co., and Shanghai Jiaerwo Co.), and a further USD 200 million from China Construction Bank (London). Supplementing Balcet's headline account with Geely Holding group financial disclosures: ZGH simultaneously mobilised a further USD 900 million in operational reserves, bringing total capital raised to approximately USD 2.7 billion; the broader financing coalition is reported to have included loans from the Bank of China and Export-Import Bank of China in addition to CCB (~USD 2.1 billion in bank credit total), equity from the listed Geely Auto arm, and contributions from the city of Gothenburg. The consequences for ZGH's balance sheet were severe: group debt surged from RMB 16.05 billion (2009) to RMB 71.07 billion (2010), pushing the debt-to-capital ratio to 73.47% — markedly above the industry high of approximately 60% — while ZGH simultaneously absorbed Volvo's pre-existing USD 3.5 billion in obligations.

The strategic logic was speed. Organic development of equivalent brand, platform, and market assets would have required decades; the acquisition compressed that timeline to a single transaction. The thesis was vindicated by Volvo Cars' IPO on Nasdaq Stockholm in October 2021, which valued the company at approximately USD 18.5 billion — more than ten times the 2010 acquisition price — while ZGH retained a ~75–80% ownership stake, realising substantial unrealised gains (Meng & Wang, 2021).

Geely's subsequent portfolio, however, carries material refinancing risk. Polestar (Nasdaq: PSNY) recorded a net loss of USD 1.17 billion in FY2023, received a going-concern qualification from auditor Deloitte, and held only USD 103.1 million in cash at end-2024; analysts estimate a further USD 2 billion in capital requirements through 2028, with ZGH committed as group-level guarantor (Geely Automobile Holdings, 2025a). Lotus Technology has accumulated a deficit of approximately USD 3.2 billion as of FY2025, with revenue falling 44% year-on-year in FY2025 (Geely Automobile Holdings, 2025b).

The contrast to peer financing models is instructive. BYD's vertically integrated model builds capability organically, avoiding acquisition debt but constraining the speed and geographic breadth of expansion. SAIC Motor's joint venture model shares capital requirements with foreign partners, limiting balance-sheet exposure while also constraining strategic independence. Geely's leverage-for-speed approach carries the highest financing risk of the three models, with group-level debt-servicing capacity remaining structurally dependent on Volvo's sustained profitability.

## Conclusion — Which Governance Model is Most Likely to Succeed in the Long Run?

The three governance models examined in this paper each embody a coherent logic suited to the conditions in which it emerged. SAIC Motor's state-directed joint venture model offered an optimal response to the technology access and market constraints of China's pre- and early-post-WTO industrial environment: foreign engineering expertise was licensed in exchange for market access, capital requirements were shared with partners, and state backing provided a sovereign backstop. BYD's vertically integrated founder-led model pursued capability through proprietary ownership rather than licensing or acquisition, constructing a self-sufficient supply chain that now spans lithium-iron-phosphate cells, silicon carbide semiconductors, and complete vehicle platforms. Geely's M&A-driven private model traded the cost efficiency of vertical ownership for the speed of strategic asset acquisition and the international legitimacy conferred by established Western brands — compressing into a single transaction the accumulated latecomer disadvantages that Luo and Tung (2007) identify as the defining constraint on emerging-market firms in global competition.

Of the three, BYD's vertically integrated model is most likely to succeed in the long run. The decisive variable is the structural economics of electrification. Battery cost constitutes the single largest determinant of EV profitability, and vertical integration over cell chemistry, manufacturing, and pack design — the core of BYD's competitive position — confers a durable cost floor that neither JV-dependent nor acquisition-dependent rivals can easily replicate. Where SAIC's electrification strategy depends on co-developed platforms whose intellectual property is shared with foreign JV partners, and where Geely's relies on platforms controlled through Volvo Cars and the Smart JV — neither of which ZGH controls unilaterally — BYD's electrification architecture is self-contained and wholly proprietary. Konda et al.'s (2022) finding that genuine technological co-creation between acquiring and acquired firms is rare among emerging-market acquirers implies that Geely's cross-acquisition platform dependencies are structurally more fragile than they appear in a benign operating environment; BYD faces no analogous dependency.

SAIC Motor's JV model faces the most acute structural headwinds. The joint venture framework was designed for a world in which technology originated in Western OEMs and market access was controlled by the Chinese state — precisely the conditions that are now reversing. Geopolitical tensions and reciprocal tariff regimes have raised the cost of the foreign partnerships on which SAIC's product portfolio depends, while domestic EV entrants with proprietary platforms compete aggressively in the segments formerly dominated by SAIC-GM and SAIC-Volkswagen. The model's core structural constraint — that strategic decisions require JV partner consent — is most disabling in periods of rapid technological discontinuity, when the speed of platform replacement determines competitive position and delay carries permanent market-share consequences.

Geely occupies an intermediate position. Its acquisition-led approach has clearly succeeded on the capability-building and financial returns dimensions: the Volvo IPO valuation of approximately USD 18.5 billion against a USD 1.5 billion acquisition price (Meng & Wang, 2021), and the NEV majority milestone of H1 2025, both confirm that the model can deliver competitive results at scale. Yet the contingent liabilities embedded in the portfolio — Polestar's going-concern qualification, Lotus Technology's accumulated deficit of approximately USD 3.2 billion, and ZGH's role as group-level guarantor for both — represent a correlated tail risk structurally absent from BYD's model. Should these liabilities crystallise simultaneously during a cyclical downturn, the leveraged balance sheet that funded Geely's strategic agility becomes its most acute vulnerability, rather than its competitive advantage.

The broader implication for Chinese automotive strategy is that the era in which governance model choice could be separated from technological trajectory is closing. The joint venture model extracted maximum value from a period of technology dependency that has largely expired; the acquisition model extracted maximum value from a period of brand and platform scarcity that is narrowing as domestic engineering capabilities converge. The vertically integrated model, by contrast, is structurally aligned with the competitive logic of electrification — proprietary control over the cost-defining component — and is therefore best positioned to generate durable returns as the industry completes its transition. The success of the acquisition and JV models was, in this reading, partly a function of their era; BYD's advantage is that its model is native to the one that follows.
