# Capital Market Financing

**A corporate financing strategy in which a firm funds growth and operations primarily through successive equity issuances in public capital markets, typically associated with pre-profitability technology companies that trade current shareholder dilution for future scale.**

## Summary

In the Chinese EV comparator framework, [[nio]] is the primary example of the capital-market-driven model. NIO has relied on repeated equity offerings since its 2018 NYSE IPO to fund R&D, manufacturing, Battery-as-a-Service infrastructure, and multi-brand expansion (ONVO, Firefly), remaining unprofitable through at least 2024 [[2026-05-22-geely-ma-strategy-web-research]].

The financial consequences are severe for existing shareholders: NIO's cash fell from RMB 32.9bn (end-2023) to RMB 19.4bn (end-2024), and the company posted a net loss of ~$3bn in 2024. Equity offerings trigger immediate stock declines (e.g., -9% on a $1bn offering announcement), reflecting the market's recognition of dilution risk [[2026-05-22-geely-ma-strategy-web-research]].

This model contrasts sharply with Geely's hybrid approach — which uses public equity sparingly (HK listing provides capital access but ZGH retains ~76% control) and relies more heavily on bank debt, government support, and seller financing for major acquisitions — and with BYD's self-funded model driven by operational cash flows from vertical integration [[2026-05-22-geely-ma-strategy-web-research]].
