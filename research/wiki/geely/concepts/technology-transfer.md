# Technology Transfer

**The process by which an acquiring firm absorbs technical knowledge, engineering capabilities, and innovation capacity from an acquired firm, enabling the acquirer to upgrade its own products and processes.**

## Summary

In the Geely-Volvo context, technology transfer is the primary mechanism through which the 2010 acquisition created long-run value for [[zhejiang-geely-holding-group]]. The transfer was not immediate or transactional but evolved across a multi-year integration process documented by Zheng, Noorderhaven & Du (2022) in four stages: distancing, balancing, building, and diversifying [[2026-05-22-geely-ma-strategy-web-research]].

Concrete mechanisms of transfer included: integrated greenfield manufacturing plants (combining Geely and Volvo production capacity); a joint engine plant; co-ordination at the supply-system level; integration of management and engineering talent; joint R&D ventures; and, most significantly, the development of the Compact Module Architecture (CMA) — a shared platform deployed across both Volvo-brand and Geely-brand vehicles [[2026-05-22-geely-ma-strategy-web-research]].

The critical design feature enabling successful transfer was the "high level of freedom given to the acquired firm" (Volvo Cars), which preserved Volvo's innovation capacity during the distancing phase. Patent portfolio analysis later showed that the two firms' innovation outputs became more similar over time, confirming successful knowledge integration (Konda, Slepnikov & Jin (2022), "From transaction to co-creation in Geely's acquisition of Volvo Cars," *Asian Journal of Technology Innovation, 31*(3), 556–580, DOI: 10.1080/19761597.2022.2133777) [[2026-05-22-geely-ma-strategy-web-research]].
