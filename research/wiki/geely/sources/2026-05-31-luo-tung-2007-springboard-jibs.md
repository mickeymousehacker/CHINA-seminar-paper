# International Expansion of Emerging Market Enterprises: A Springboard Perspective (2007)

**Foundational theoretical paper introducing the "springboard perspective" to explain why and how emerging-market multinational enterprises (EMNEs) use cross-border acquisitions of developed-market firms as springboards to overcome latecomer disadvantages and accelerate internationalisation.**

## Key takeaways
- Emerging-market firms face institutional and resource disadvantages relative to incumbent multinationals; cross-border M&A of developed-market assets provides a shortcut to closing these gaps.
- The "springboard" metaphor captures the logic: the acquirer uses the acquired firm's brand, technology, and distribution as a launch platform for global competitiveness.
- The theory distinguishes EMNEs from traditional FDI theory (OLI/eclectic paradigm): EMNEs often acquire abroad before they have fully developed ownership advantages at home.
- Internationalisation for EMNEs is simultaneously a means of asset augmentation (gaining capabilities) and asset exploitation (leveraging home-market scale).

## Claims
- EMNEs use outward FDI as a springboard to acquire strategic resources and reduce home-country institutional and market constraints.
- Cross-border acquisitions by EMNEs are driven by asset-seeking rather than market-seeking or efficiency-seeking motives.
- The springboard perspective predicts that EMNEs will target firms in advanced economies with technology, brand, or distribution assets the acquirer lacks.
- Risk-taking is rational for EMNEs because the alternative — slow organic catch-up — forfeits first-mover advantages in global competition.
